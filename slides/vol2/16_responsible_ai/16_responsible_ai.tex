\begin{frame}{Three Definitions of Fairness}
\note{
% -- LINK: What prior concept connects to this slide
Students wrote conflicting definitions. This slide reveals that three formal
metrics exist --- and they capture different intuitions about fairness.

% -- NARRATE: What to SAY while showing this slide
[3 min] Walk through the diagram: ``Demographic Parity: equal approval rates
regardless of ground truth. Equalized Odds: equal error rates conditioned on
the true label. Equality of Opportunity: equal TPR among qualified applicants.''
Use the loan example to make distinctions concrete.

% -- WARN: What students will get wrong on THIS topic
Common error: students think one metric implies the others. Correct framing:
these metrics conflict mathematically --- satisfying one can violate another.

% -- FLEX: [CORE] + contingency
[CORE] The three metrics are the foundation for the impossibility theorem.
}

\centering

\vspace{0.1cm}

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Fairness Metrics: The Math}
\note{
% -- LINK: What prior concept connects to this slide
The diagram showed the intuitions. This slide provides the formal math ---
precise definitions students can implement.

% -- NARRATE: What to SAY while showing this slide
[3 min] Walk through each equation: ``h(x) predicts, S is sensitive attribute,
Y is true label. Demographic Parity ignores Y entirely. Equalized Odds
conditions on Y for both classes. Equality of Opportunity conditions on Y=1
only.'' Read the impossibility card: ``When base rates differ, no classifier
satisfies all three simultaneously.''

% -- WARN: What students will get wrong on THIS topic
Common error: students think calibration + equalized odds is achievable.
Correct framing: Kleinberg et al.\ proved this is mathematically impossible
when base rates differ between groups.

% -- FLEX: [CORE] + contingency
[CORE] The formal definitions enable the worked exercise on the next active
learning slide. IF SHORT: Show equations 1 and 2 and the impossibility card.
}

\footnotesize
\textbf{Let} $h(x)$ predict outcome, $S$ = sensitive attribute, $Y$ = true label.

\vspace{0.05cm}
\textbf{1. Demographic Parity} (equal approval rates):
\vspace{-0.1cm}
$$P\big(h(x)=1 \mid S=a\big) = P\big(h(x)=1 \mid S=b\big)$$

\vspace{-0.15cm}
\textbf{2. Equalized Odds} (equal TPR \emph{and} FPR):
\vspace{-0.1cm}
$$P\big(h(x)=1 \mid S=a, Y=y\big) = P\big(h(x)=1 \mid S=b, Y=y\big), \;\; y \in \{0,1\}$$

\vspace{-0.15cm}
\textbf{3. Equality of Opportunity} (equal TPR only):
\vspace{-0.1cm}
$$P\big(h(x)=1 \mid S=a, Y=1\big) = P\big(h(x)=1 \mid S=b, Y=1\big)$$

\vspace{-0.1cm}
\begin{mlsyscard}{errorstroke}
{\scriptsize \textbf{Impossibility} (Kleinberg et al., 2016): When base rates differ, no classifier satisfies all three simultaneously.}
\end{mlsyscard}

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{The Fairness Tax}
\note{
% -- LINK: What prior concept connects to this slide
The impossibility theorem says you cannot have all metrics. The fairness tax
quantifies the cost of choosing one.

% -- NARRATE: What to SAY while showing this slide
[3 min] Walk through the diagram: ``Unconstrained: 85\% accuracy, Group A 80\%
approval, Group B 60\%. Enforce demographic parity: approve Group B at 80\%
too. This forces approving risky applicants. New accuracy: 81\%. The 4\% drop
is the Fairness Tax.'' Read the crimson card: ``Engineers must budget this
trade-off, not pretend it does not exist.''

% -- WARN: What students will get wrong on THIS topic
Common error: students think 4\% accuracy loss is negligible. Correct framing:
in credit scoring, 4\% accuracy drop means millions of dollars in additional
defaults --- this is a real business cost that must be explicitly accepted.

% -- FLEX: [CORE] + contingency
[CORE] The 4\% Fairness Tax is the key quantitative anchor.
}

\centering

\section{Feedback Loops}
% =============================================================================

\begin{frame}{Feedback Loops Amplify Bias}
\note{
% -- LINK: What prior concept connects to this slide
Fairness metrics measure a snapshot. Feedback loops show how bias compounds
over time --- the system destabilizes its own data distribution.

% -- NARRATE: What to SAY while showing this slide
[3 min] Walk through the diagram: ``Historical data over-represents certain
neighborhoods. Model predicts high crime there. More patrols sent. More arrests
made. Training data reinforced. The cycle amplifies the original bias.''
Key insight: ``Bias is a distributional instability --- the system corrupts

\begin{frame}{Machine Unlearning: The Right to Be Forgotten}
\note{
% -- LINK: What prior concept connects to this slide
The Privacy Preservation slide introduced machine unlearning briefly. This
dedicated slide covers the systems engineering challenge in depth --- GDPR
Article 17 makes this a legal requirement, not an optional feature.

% -- NARRATE: What to SAY while showing this slide
[3 min] Walk through the three approaches: ``Exact unlearning: retrain from
scratch without the user's data. Gold standard but costs 100\% of original
training compute. Approximate: use influence functions to estimate and remove
a user's contribution. 1,000--10,000x cheaper but provides only statistical
guarantees. SISA training: partition data into shards so unlearning only
requires retraining the affected shard --- 10--100x cheaper than full retrain.''
Key punchline: ``A 175B model costs \$4M to train. Exact unlearning for each
GDPR request at \$4M is not viable --- approximate methods are essential.''

% -- ENGAGE: Specific question, prediction, or task for THIS slide
Ask: ``If you get 100 GDPR deletion requests per month, what is the annual cost
of exact unlearning for a \$4M model?'' Give 10 seconds.
[Expected: 100 x 12 x \$4M = \$4.8B --- obviously absurd. This motivates
approximate methods.]

% -- WARN: What students will get wrong on THIS topic
Common error: students think deleting the user's data from the dataset is
sufficient. Correct framing: the model has already learned from that data ---
deletion from the dataset does not remove the influence from the weights.

% -- FLEX: [CORE] + contingency

    \textbf{SISA Training}\\[0.05cm]
    {\scriptsize
    \textbf{S}harded, \textbf{I}solated, \textbf{S}liced, \textbf{A}ggregated:\\[0.05cm]

  \end{column}
\end{columns}

\begin{frame}{Fallacies}
\note{
% -- NARRATE: What to SAY while showing this slide
[2 min] Read each fallacy. The healthcare algorithm fallacy is most impactful:
it affected 200M Americans --- the issue was proxy selection, not data quantity.

\footnotesize
\textbf{Fallacy:} \textit{Bias can be eliminated through better algorithms and more data.}\\
{\scriptsize The healthcare algorithm affected 200M Americans; the issue was proxy selection, not data quantity. Impossibility theorems prove no algorithm satisfies all fairness criteria simultaneously.}

\vspace{0.08cm}
\textbf{Fallacy:} \textit{Achieving one fairness metric guarantees overall fairness.}\\
{\scriptsize Demographic parity forces calibration violations. Single-metric optimization exposes organizations to litigation on other legally relevant criteria.}

\vspace{0.08cm}
\textbf{Fallacy:} \textit{Responsible AI practices impose only costs without business value.}\\
{\scriptsize Differential privacy enables use of legally restricted data. Bias monitoring detected the healthcare algorithm's 50\% enrollment reduction before regulatory intervention.}

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Pitfalls}
\note{
% -- NARRATE: What to SAY while showing this slide
[2 min] Read each pitfall. The first: SHAP adds 50--200 ms, violating 100 ms
SLA --- explainability must be budgeted at architecture time. The second:
group-specific thresholds destroy calibration. The third: governance tied to
individual champions fails when they leave.

% -- FLEX: [CORE] + contingency
[CORE] Pitfalls are operational guidance.
IF SHORT: Cover the first pitfall (explainability as architecture decision).
}

\footnotesize
\textbf{Pitfall:} \textit{Treating explainability as an optional post-deployment feature.}\\
SHAP adds 50--200 ms per request. A 100 ms SLA system cannot retrofit SHAP without 5$\times$ infrastructure cost. Explainability must be budgeted at architecture time.

\vspace{0.15cm}
\textbf{Pitfall:} \textit{Implementing fairness via post-hoc threshold adjustment.}\\
Group-specific thresholds destroy calibration: ``80\% confidence'' means different things for different groups. Proper approach: jointly optimize during training, accepting accuracy-fairness trade-offs.

\vspace{0.15cm}
\textbf{Pitfall:} \textit{Assuming governance structures survive without institutional embedding.}\\
Responsible AI tied to individual champions fails when they leave. Governance must be embedded in CI/CD pipelines, deployment gates, and organizational incentive structures.

\end{frame}
