\small
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Calculate system-level \textbf{MTBF} from component failure rates and derive optimal checkpoint intervals using the \textbf{Young-Daly formula}
  \item Design distributed \textbf{checkpoint and recovery} strategies for multi-terabyte model states across thousands of GPUs
  \item Implement serving \textbf{redundancy}, \textbf{replication}, and \textbf{failover} within millisecond latency requirements

  \item Construct \textbf{observability infrastructure} using metrics, logs, and traces to diagnose non-deterministic failures
  \item Evaluate fault tolerance trade-offs between \textbf{training} and \textbf{serving} workloads

\begin{frame}{The ML Failure Spectrum}
\note{
% -- LINK: What prior concept connects to this slide
Students understand distributed training from Ch 5--6 but assume
hardware is reliable. This slide reveals the three categories of
failure they will encounter in production.

% -- NARRATE: What to SAY while showing this slide
Point to each band in the diagram left-to-right: ``Hard failures are
obvious --- the machine crashes, the job dies. Partial failures are
subtle --- one GPU returns wrong gradients but the job continues.
Silent data corruption is the most dangerous because no error is

ANALOGY: ``Hard failure is a broken bone. Silent corruption is cancer

there is no error signal.

In reality, silent data corruption (bit flips, NaN propagation)
causes the most expensive incidents because it goes undetected for

\begin{frame}{Hardware Fault Taxonomy}
\note{
% -- LINK: What prior concept connects to this slide
The failure spectrum gave three broad categories. This slide expands the hardware fault taxonomy into four precise types with quantitative fault rates.

% -- NARRATE: What to SAY while showing this slide
Walk through the table: ``Transient faults: cosmic ray bit flips, 1 per 10K GPU-hours. They vanish on retry but corrupt results silently. Permanent faults: dead GPU, dead NIC --- obvious and detectable. Intermittent faults: the worst --- a GPU that works 99\% of the time but produces wrong results under thermal stress. Byzantine faults: a component that lies --- sends incorrect data while appearing healthy. At scale, all four types occur daily.''

% -- ENGAGE: Specific question, prediction, or task for THIS slide
Ask: ``At 10,000 GPUs, how many transient faults per day?'' Expected: 10,000 / 10,000 = ~1 per hour, or ~24 per day.

% -- WARN: What students will get wrong on THIS topic
Students think transient means harmless. A single bit flip in a gradient accumulator can cause a NaN that propagates through the entire model within minutes.

% -- FLEX: [CORE] Establishes precise vocabulary for the rest of the chapter.
IF SHORT: Focus on transient and intermittent only; skip Byzantine.
}

\footnotesize
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.15}
\begin{tabular}{@{}llrl@{}}
  \toprule
  \textbf{Fault Type} & \textbf{Behavior} & \textbf{Rate} & \textbf{Detection} \\
  \midrule
  \textbf{Transient}    & Vanishes on retry (bit flip)         & 1 per 10K GPU-h  & \textcolor{errorstroke}{Silent} \\
  \textbf{Permanent}    & Component dead (GPU, NIC, disk)      & 1 per 50K GPU-h  & \textcolor{datastroke}{ECC/heartbeat} \\
  \textbf{Intermittent} & Works 99\% of the time                & 1 per 25K GPU-h  & \textcolor{errorstroke}{Very hard} \\

\begin{frame}{Software Faults and the Bathtub Curve}
\note{
% -- LINK: What prior concept connects to this slide
Hardware faults are physics. Software faults are engineering --- and they cause 30--40\% of all ML training interruptions.

% -- NARRATE: What to SAY while showing this slide
Point to the bathtub curve: ``Left: infant mortality --- configuration errors, library version mismatches, driver bugs. These dominate the first 24--48 hours of a new training run. Middle: the stable period with random hardware failures. Right: wear-out --- memory leaks, log file accumulation, thermal degradation. The economic question: at what point does fault tolerance investment pay for itself? If your cluster costs \$10M/month and 30\% of time is wasted on failures, fault tolerance worth \$3M/month in recovered compute.''

% -- ENGAGE: Specific question, prediction, or task for THIS slide
Ask: ``Which phase of the bathtub curve do most student projects experience?'' Expected: infant mortality --- most projects never run long enough to see wear-out.

% -- WARN: What students will get wrong on THIS topic
Students focus exclusively on hardware. Software faults (OOM from batch accumulation, NCCL timeout from network jitter, checkpoint corruption from race conditions) are more common and harder to reproduce.

% -- FLEX: [OPTIONAL] Supplements the hardware taxonomy.
IF SHORT: State ``software causes 30--40\% of failures'' and skip the bathtub curve.
}

\begin{frame}{The Mathematics of Inevitable Failure}
\note{
% -- LINK: What prior concept connects to this slide
The failure spectrum showed qualitative categories. Now we quantify:
how often does the system fail as a function of cluster size?

% -- NARRATE: What to SAY while showing this slide
Point to the reliability product rule: ``Each component has exponential
reliability. For N components in series, failure rates add. The system
MTBF is the component MTBF divided by N.'' Then point to the table:
``At 8 GPUs you get months between failures. At 10,000 GPUs, you fail
every 5 hours. At 25,000 GPUs, every 2 hours.''
ANALOGY: ``One light bulb lasts a year. A stadium with 10,000 bulbs
has one blow out every hour.''

% -- ENGAGE: Specific question, prediction, or task for THIS slide
Before showing the table, ask: ``If one GPU fails once per year
(50,000 hours MTBF), how often does a 10,000-GPU cluster fail?''
Give 15 seconds. Expected answer: 50000/10000 = 5 hours.

% -- WARN: What students will get wrong on THIS topic
Students think adding redundancy always helps. Redundancy helps
individual components but the system MTBF formula is 1/N --- adding
more GPUs makes the system fail MORE often, not less.

% -- FLEX: [CORE] or [OPTIONAL] + contingency
[CORE] The MTBF/N equation is the central insight of the chapter.
IF AHEAD: ``What happens to this formula with correlated failures?''
}

\small
\begin{columns}[T]
  \begin{column}{0.52\textwidth}
    Individual component reliability:
    $$R_{single}(t) = e^{-\lambda t}$$

    \vspace{0.1cm}
    For $N$ identical components (series):
    $$R_{system}(t) = e^{-N\lambda t}$$

    \vspace{0.1cm}
    \begin{mlsyscard}{crimson}
    \textbf{System MTBF:}
    $$\MTBF_{system} = \frac{\MTBF_{component}}{N}$$
    \end{mlsyscard}
  \end{column}
  \begin{column}{0.44\textwidth}
    \vspace{0.2cm}
    \mlsysconcept{Scale inverts reliability:}{}

    \vspace{0.1cm}
    {\footnotesize
    \renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.15}
    \begin{tabular}{@{}rr@{}}
      \toprule
      \textbf{GPUs} & \textbf{Cluster MTBF} \\
      \midrule
      8       & 260 days \\
      1,000   & 2 days \\
      10,000  & \textbf{5 hours} \\
      25,000  & \textbf{2 hours} \\
      \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
    }

    \vspace{0.1cm}
    {\footnotesize\textcolor{midgray}{Per-GPU MTBF: 50,000 hours}}
  \end{column}
\end{columns}

\end{frame}

\section{The Young-Daly Law}
% =============================================================================

\mlsysfocus{The Young-Daly Law}{%
$\tau_{\text{opt}} = \sqrt{2 \cdot T_{\text{write}} \cdot \MTBF}$%
}

\begin{frame}{Optimal Checkpoint Interval}
\note{
% -- LINK: What prior concept connects to this slide
The prediction exercise showed that failures are frequent. The
Young-Daly formula tells us exactly how often to checkpoint.

% -- NARRATE: What to SAY while showing this slide
Point to the U-shaped curve: ``Left side: too frequent checkpoints
waste I/O time writing state. Right side: too infrequent checkpoints
waste rework after each failure. The minimum is the Young-Daly
optimum --- the square root of twice the write time times MTBF.''

% -- ENGAGE: Specific question, prediction, or task for THIS slide
Ask: ``Which side of the curve is worse --- too frequent or too rare?''
Expected: too rare is worse because the rework cost after failure is
unbounded, while I/O overhead is linear.

% -- WARN: What students will get wrong on THIS topic
Students guess checkpoint intervals by intuition (``every hour'')
instead of computing the optimum. The formula often gives surprising
answers (e.g., 27 minutes, not 1 hour).

% -- FLEX: [CORE] or [OPTIONAL] + contingency
[CORE] The Young-Daly formula is tested in problem sets.
IF AHEAD: ``What happens to tau\_opt when you halve the write time?''
(Answer: decreases by factor of sqrt(2), not 2.)
}

\centering
\safeimg[width=0.88\textwidth,height=4.0cm,keepaspectratio]{young-daly-tradeoff.pdf}

\vspace{0.1cm}

    \centering

\begin{frame}{Young-Daly: Worked Example}
\note{
% -- LINK: What prior concept connects to this slide
The U-shaped curve showed the trade-off qualitatively. Now we plug
in real Llama-3 numbers to see the formula in action.

% -- NARRATE: What to SAY while showing this slide
Walk through step by step: ``Checkpoint write time is 2 minutes.
Cluster MTBF at 16K GPUs is about 3 hours or 180 minutes. Plug in:
sqrt(2 times 2 times 180) = sqrt(720) = 27 minutes.'' Then compare
to the overhead percentages: 10 min = 17\%, 60 min = 15\%, 27 min = 7\%.
Note the square-root relationship: halving MTBF only increases
frequency by 1.4x, not 2x.

% -- WARN: What students will get wrong on THIS topic
Students expect the optimal to be ``halfway between too frequent and
too rare.'' The square root makes it much closer to the frequent side.

% -- FLEX: [CORE] or [OPTIONAL] + contingency
[CORE] Worked example that anchors the formula.
IF SHORT: Just show the final result (27 min) and the overhead table.
}

\small
\textbf{Scenario: Llama-3 training on 16,000 H100 GPUs}

\vspace{0.15cm}

{\footnotesize
\begin{tabular}{@{}lll@{}}

  \textbf{Parameter} & \textbf{Value} & \textbf{Source} \\

\section{Checkpointing}
% =============================================================================

\begin{frame}{Checkpoint-Recovery Timeline}
\note{
% -- LINK: What prior concept connects to this slide
Young-Daly told us WHEN to checkpoint. This Gantt chart shows WHAT
happens during the checkpoint-failure-recovery cycle.

% -- NARRATE: What to SAY while showing this slide
Walk through the timeline left-to-right: ``Productive training runs
until the checkpoint write (blue pause). Training resumes. Then failure
strikes (red X). All work since the last checkpoint is lost --- on
average tau/2. Recovery loads the checkpoint and warms up. Total
failure cost = lost work + recovery latency.''

% -- ENGAGE: Specific question, prediction, or task for THIS slide
Ask: ``What determines recovery latency --- the checkpoint size or the
storage bandwidth?'' Expected: both --- it is checkpoint size divided
by read bandwidth, plus warmup time. Restart can take 3--5x the
checkpoint write time.

% -- WARN: What students will get wrong on THIS topic
Students forget that restart overhead includes warmup (re-priming
data loaders, re-building communication groups), not just loading
weights. Total cost is higher than just ``read the file.''

% -- FLEX: [CORE] or [OPTIONAL] + contingency
[CORE] The timeline is referenced throughout checkpointing discussion.
IF SHORT: Focus on the ``lost work = tau/2'' insight and skip warmup details.
}

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Checkpoint State: What Gets Saved?}
\note{
% -- LINK: What prior concept connects to this slide
The timeline showed checkpoint writes as brief pauses. This slide
reveals why those writes are expensive: the state is massive.

% -- NARRATE: What to SAY while showing this slide
Walk through the left column: ``Model weights in FP16 are 2 bytes per
parameter. Adam optimizer stores m and v at 4 bytes each. Total: 12
bytes per parameter. For GPT-3 at 175B params, that is 2.1 terabytes.''
Then the right column: ``When 10K GPUs write simultaneously, you get a
checkpoint storm --- network and file system saturated.'' Walk through
the three mitigations: async, tiered, sharded.

% -- WARN: What students will get wrong on THIS topic
Students compute checkpoint size as just the model weights (2 bytes/param).
The optimizer state (m + v) doubles the naive expectation to 12
bytes/param total.

% -- FLEX: [CORE] or [OPTIONAL] + contingency
[CORE] The 2.1 TB number motivates async checkpointing.
IF SHORT: Focus on the GPT-3 size and the checkpoint storm concept.
Skip the three mitigation strategies --- they appear in the backup slide.
}

\footnotesize
\begin{columns}[T]
  \begin{column}{0.50\textwidth}
    \textbf{Full training state:}
    \begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{0pt}
      \item Model weights (FP16): 2 bytes/param
      \item Adam $m$ (first moment): 4 bytes/param
      \item Adam $v$ (second moment): 4 bytes/param
      \item Data loader position, LR schedule
      \item Random seeds for reproducibility
    \end{itemize}

    \vspace{0.1cm}
    \textbf{Total}: 12 bytes/param

    \vspace{0.1cm}
    \begin{mlsyscard}{errorstroke}
    {\footnotesize \textbf{GPT-3} (175B params):\\
    Full checkpoint $\approx$ \textbf{2.1 TB}}
    \end{mlsyscard}
  \end{column}
  \begin{column}{0.46\textwidth}
    \textbf{The Checkpoint Storm:}

    \vspace{0.1cm}
    When 10,000 GPUs write simultaneously:
    \begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{0pt}
      \item Network fabric saturated
      \item Parallel file system overwhelmed
      \item I/O contention cascades
    \end{itemize}

    \vspace{0.1cm}
    \textbf{Mitigation strategies:}
    \begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{0pt}
      \item \textcolor{computestroke}{Async checkpointing}: write in background
      \item \textcolor{datastroke}{Tiered staging}: GPU $\to$ CPU $\to$ NVMe $\to$ PFS
      \item \textcolor{routingstroke}{Sharded writes}: each rank saves its shard
    \end{itemize}
  \end{column}
\end{columns}

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Elastic Training: Dynamic Recovery}
\note{
% -- LINK: What prior concept connects to this slide
Checkpointing saves state but still requires a full restart. Elastic

% -- NARRATE: What to SAY while showing this slide

\begin{frame}{Circuit Breaker Pattern}
\note{
% -- LINK: What prior concept connects to this slide
Graceful degradation showed intentional quality reduction. The circuit
breaker is the mechanism that triggers it automatically --- preventing
one failing dependency from cascading across the fleet.

% -- NARRATE: What to SAY while showing this slide
Point to the three states: ``Closed is normal --- requests pass through.
When error rate exceeds a threshold, the breaker Opens --- requests are
rejected immediately, preventing the failing service from exhausting
connection pools. After a cooldown, it enters Half-Open and sends a
probe request to test if the dependency recovered.'' Named after
electrical circuit breakers that protect wiring from overload.

% -- WARN: What students will get wrong on THIS topic
Students think circuit breakers just retry. The Open state does the
opposite: it STOPS retrying. Without this, retries from thousands of
clients amplify a small failure into a fleet-wide cascade.

\begin{frame}{Three Pillars of Observability}
\note{
% -- LINK: What prior concept connects to this slide
Fault tolerance requires detection before recovery. The previous
sections assumed failures are detected. This section asks: how?

% -- NARRATE: What to SAY while showing this slide
Point to the three pillars in the diagram: ``Metrics tell you WHEN
something went wrong --- a latency spike at 3:42 PM. Logs tell you
WHAT happened --- GPU 7 in node 42 reported an ECC error. Traces tell
you WHY --- the error propagated through AllReduce to corrupt
gradients on 8 other ranks.'' All three must be correlated to diagnose
distributed failures.

% -- ENGAGE: Specific question, prediction, or task for THIS slide
Ask: ``Why can you not just add print statements to debug a distributed
training failure?'' Expected: Heisenbugs --- adding instrumentation
changes timing, which can mask the race condition. The bug disappears
when you try to observe it.

% -- WARN: What students will get wrong on THIS topic
Students think observability means ``more logging.'' In distributed
systems, unstructured logging creates noise. Structured, correlated
observability (metrics + logs + traces with shared request IDs) is
what enables diagnosis.

[CORE] Observability underpins all detection and recovery strategies.

}

\begin{frame}{Fallacies}
\note{
% -- LINK: What prior concept connects to this slide
The chapter covered training and serving fault tolerance. These
fallacies challenge common beliefs students may still hold.

% -- NARRATE: What to SAY while showing this slide
Read each fallacy, then the rebuttal. Emphasize the numbers:
``Hardware failures are only 15--25\% of incidents. Software bugs
cause 30--40\%. Silent corruption at 0.01--0.1\% of GPUs per year
means 1--10 silent events per year at 10K GPUs. And elastic training
without checkpoints fails below minimum worker count.''

% -- FLEX: [CORE] or [OPTIONAL] + contingency
[CORE] Fallacies reinforce quantitative reasoning from the chapter.
IF SHORT: Cover only the first fallacy (hardware vs. software causes).
}

\footnotesize
\textbf{Fallacy:} \textit{Hardware failures are the main concern.}\\
Software bugs cause 30--40\% of incidents, config errors 20--30\%. Hardware is only 15--25\%. Hardware redundancy alone leaves most failure modes unaddressed.

\vspace{0.1cm}
\textbf{Fallacy:} \textit{Silent data corruption is rare in modern hardware.}\\
0.01--0.1\% of GPUs per year exhibit silent corruption not caught by ECC. For 10K GPUs: 1--10 silent events/year, hundreds of bit flips monthly. Gradient NaNs blamed on LR may be bit flips.

\vspace{0.1cm}
\textbf{Fallacy:} \textit{Elastic training eliminates the need for checkpointing.}\\
Elasticity cannot survive below-minimum worker count, accumulated degradation, or software bugs in remaining workers. Elastic + checkpoint is the answer.

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Pitfalls}
\note{
% -- LINK: What prior concept connects to this slide
Fallacies addressed misconceptions. Pitfalls address operational
mistakes students will make when implementing fault tolerance.

% -- NARRATE: What to SAY while showing this slide
``First pitfall: setting checkpoint interval by intuition. We showed
that `every hour' can be 2--3x off from optimal. Use the formula.
Second: ignoring restart overhead. A 5-min save plus 20-min restart
means 25-min effective cost, not 5. Third: testing fault tolerance
only during real failures. Chaos engineering lets you discover problems
before production does.''

% -- FLEX: [CORE] or [OPTIONAL] + contingency
[CORE] Operational pitfalls that students will encounter in practice.
IF SHORT: Cover only the checkpoint interval pitfall.
}

\footnotesize
\textbf{Pitfall:} \textit{Setting checkpoint interval by intuition.}\\
{\scriptsize ``Every hour'' may be 2--3$\times$ off. For MTBF=4h, $T_w$=5min: $\tau_{opt}=49$ min. If $T_w$=15 min: $\tau_{opt}=85$ min, not ``every 15 min.''}

\vspace{0.1cm}
\textbf{Pitfall:} \textit{Ignoring restart overhead in checkpoint planning.}\\
{\scriptsize 5-min save + 20-min restart = 25-min effective cost, not 5. Use $T_{save} + T_{restart}$ in the formula.}

\vspace{0.1cm}
\textbf{Pitfall:} \textit{Testing fault tolerance only during real failures.}\\
{\scriptsize Checkpoint restoration and circuit breakers may be untested. \textbf{Chaos engineering} transforms ``we think it works'' into ``we know it works.''}

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Key Takeaways}
\note{
% -- LINK: What prior concept connects to this slide
Students just attempted retrieval. Now reveal the answers so they
can compare and self-correct.

% -- NARRATE: What to SAY while showing this slide
Walk through each bullet: ``MTBF/N is the central equation. Young-Daly
governs checkpointing. The checkpoint storm problem is 2.1 TB for
GPT-3. Elastic training converts failures to throughput drops. Training
needs minutes of recovery while serving needs milliseconds. 75\% quality
at 100\% availability beats the alternative. And observability gives
you metrics (when), logs (what), and traces (why).''

% -- FLEX: [CORE] or [OPTIONAL] + contingency
[CORE] Summary slide after retrieval practice.
IF SHORT: Read bullets quickly without elaboration.
}

\scriptsize
\begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{0pt}
  \item \textbf{Scale guarantees failure}: $\MTBF_{sys} = \MTBF_{comp}/N$. A 10K-GPU cluster fails every 5 hours.
  \item \textbf{Young-Daly governs checkpoints}: $\tau_{opt} = \sqrt{2 \cdot T_w \cdot \MTBF}$. Typical: 20--30 min.
  \item \textbf{Checkpoint storm}: GPT-3 = 2.1 TB. Async + tiered staging prevents saturation.
  \item \textbf{Elastic training}: Convert fatal failures to throughput drops via batch/LR/shard adjustment.
  \item \textbf{Training vs serving}: Minutes of recovery vs millisecond failover.
  \item \textbf{Graceful degradation}: 75\% quality at 100\% availability $>$ 100\% quality at 0\%.
  \item \textbf{Observability}: Metrics (when) + Logs (what) + Traces (why) = distributed debugging.
\end{itemize}

\end{frame}

\footnotesize
\textbf{Three-phase async checkpoint:}
\begin{enumerate}\setlength\itemsep{1pt}
  \item \textbf{Snapshot}: Copy GPU state to pinned CPU memory (ms)
  \item \textbf{Background write}: CPU writes to NVMe/PFS (seconds)
  \item \textbf{Replication}: Async copy to remote storage (minutes)
\end{enumerate}
