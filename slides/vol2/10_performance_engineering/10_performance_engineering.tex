\section{Memory Wall}
% =============================================================================

\begin{frame}{The Memory Wall}
\note{
% -- LINK: What prior concept connects to this slide
Ch 9 showed that LLM decode is memory-bandwidth-bound. This slide
reveals the physics behind that observation: the Memory Wall.

% -- NARRATE: What to SAY while showing this slide
Point to the diagram: ``An H100 has 989 TFLOPS of compute but only
3.35 TB/s of memory bandwidth. During LLM decode, the GPU loads the
full weight matrix from HBM for each token --- one matrix-vector
multiply. The arithmetic intensity is about 2 FLOP/byte. The GPU
sits 95\% idle waiting for data.''
ANALOGY: ``A factory with 1000 workers but only one loading dock.
The workers stand idle waiting for materials.''

% -- ENGAGE: Specific question, prediction, or task for THIS slide
Ask: ``If the GPU has 989 TFLOPS, why does LLM decode use only 2\%?''
Expected: because each weight byte produces only 2 FLOPs. The
bottleneck is getting weights from HBM, not computing with them.

% -- WARN: What students will get wrong on THIS topic
Students think the memory wall is an engineering failure that will be
fixed. It is physics: moving data costs 1,280x more energy from HBM
than from SRAM. Every GPU generation makes it worse.

% -- FLEX: [CORE] or [OPTIONAL] + contingency
[CORE] The memory wall is the central insight of the chapter.
IF SHORT: Show diagram and the 1,280x energy number.
}

\centering

\vspace{0.15cm}
\mlsysalert{Key:}{Moving data costs 1,280$\times$ more energy from HBM than from SRAM. This is physics, not engineering.}

\end{frame}

\section{Roofline Model}
% =============================================================================

\begin{frame}{The Roofline Model}
\note{
% -- LINK: What prior concept connects to this slide
The memory wall showed the problem qualitatively. The roofline model
provides the quantitative diagnostic framework.

% -- NARRATE: What to SAY while showing this slide
Point to the diagram: ``Two ceilings form a roofline. The sloped line
is the bandwidth ceiling: performance = AI times bandwidth. The flat
line is the compute ceiling: peak FLOPS. They meet at the ridge point.
If your workload's arithmetic intensity falls left of the ridge, you
are memory-bound --- faster compute yields zero improvement.''

% -- ENGAGE: Specific question, prediction, or task for THIS slide
Ask: ``If AI < ridge, what happens when you upgrade to a GPU with 2x
FLOPS?'' Expected: nothing --- you are bandwidth-bound. Only
increasing bandwidth or reducing bytes moved helps.

% -- WARN: What students will get wrong on THIS topic
Students think more FLOPS always helps. The roofline shows that for
memory-bound workloads (most transformer inference), FLOPS upgrades
are wasted money.

% -- FLEX: [CORE] or [OPTIONAL] + contingency

\section{Operator Fusion}
% =============================================================================

\begin{frame}{Operator Fusion: Before and After}
\note{
% -- LINK: What prior concept connects to this slide
The roofline showed most operations are memory-bound. Operator fusion
reduces bytes moved by keeping intermediate results in SRAM.

% -- NARRATE: What to SAY while showing this slide
Point to left (unfused) side of the diagram: ``The naive path writes
intermediate tensor Z1 to HBM after bias, reads it back for GELU,
writes Z2, reads for dropout. Five HBM round trips per layer.'' Then
the right (fused) side: ``The fused kernel keeps everything in SRAM.
Two HBM accesses instead of five. That saves 32 MB of redundant
traffic per layer.''

% -- WARN: What students will get wrong on THIS topic
Students think fusion only helps for small operations. In a 70-layer
model, 32 MB per layer times 70 layers = 2.2 GB of wasted traffic
eliminated per forward pass.

% -- FLEX: [CORE] or [OPTIONAL] + contingency
[CORE] Fusion is the most broadly applicable optimization technique.
IF SHORT: Show the diagram and state the 5-to-2 reduction.
}

% --- Full-width diagram ---
\centering
\safeimg[width=0.92\textwidth,height=4.8cm,keepaspectratio]{operator-fusion.pdf}

\vspace{0.1cm}
\mlsysinsight{Impact:}{Fusion reduces HBM transfers from 5 to 2 per layer, saving 32 MB of redundant traffic.}

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{FlashAttention: Tiled Attention}
\note{
% -- LINK: What prior concept connects to this slide
Fusion categories showed FlashAttention as the highest-impact fusion.
This slide explains why it matters and how it works.

% -- NARRATE: What to SAY while showing this slide
``Standard attention materializes an N-by-N matrix in HBM: 128 MB per
head at sequence length 8192. FlashAttention tiles the computation so
this matrix never leaves SRAM. Online softmax makes this exact, not
approximate --- the output is bit-identical. Result: 65x reduction in
HBM traffic.'' Point to the key innovation line.

% -- ENGAGE: Specific question, prediction, or task for THIS slide
Ask: ``Why can we not just use more HBM?'' Expected: the N\^2 matrix
grows quadratically with sequence length. At 128K tokens, it would be
32 GB per head --- impossible regardless of HBM size.

% -- WARN: What students will get wrong on THIS topic
Students think FlashAttention is an approximation. It is exact ---
online softmax produces the same numerical result. The innovation is
algorithmic (tiling with running statistics), not approximation.

% -- FLEX: [CORE] or [OPTIONAL] + contingency
[CORE] FlashAttention is the most important kernel optimization in modern ML.
IF SHORT: State ``exact, 65x HBM reduction via tiling + online softmax.''
}

% --- Full-width diagram ---
\centering
\safeimg[width=0.92\textwidth,height=4.2cm,keepaspectratio]{flashattention.pdf}

\vspace{0.05cm}
{\footnotesize\textbf{Key Innovation:} Online softmax computes the \emph{exact} result tile-by-tile, never materializing $N \times N$.}

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{CUDA Graphs: Eliminating Launch Overhead}
\note{
% -- LINK: What prior concept connects to this slide
FlashAttention eliminates memory round-trips. But even with fused kernels, each kernel launch costs 5--15 microseconds of CPU-side overhead. For small decode batches, launch overhead can exceed kernel execution time.

% -- NARRATE: What to SAY while showing this slide
``A single LLM decode step launches 30--100 kernels. At 10 microseconds each, that is 300--1000 microseconds of pure overhead --- comparable to the compute itself at batch=1. CUDA Graphs capture the entire kernel sequence once, then replay it as a single GPU operation. Launch overhead drops from 300--1000 microseconds to under 10 microseconds.'' Point to the comparison: ``Before: CPU dispatches each kernel sequentially with gaps. After: the entire graph replays as one unit with no gaps.''

% -- ENGAGE: Specific question, prediction, or task for THIS slide
Ask: ``When does CUDA Graph overhead matter most?'' Expected: at batch=1 decode, where kernels are small and launch overhead dominates. At batch=256, kernels are large enough that launch overhead is negligible.

% -- WARN: What students will get wrong on THIS topic
Students think CUDA Graphs speed up the kernels themselves. They do not --- they eliminate the gaps between kernels. The speedup is proportional to the ratio of gap time to kernel time.

% -- FLEX: [OPTIONAL] Complements FlashAttention with a different optimization target.
IF SHORT: State the 30--100 kernel launches and the 10 microsecond replay result.
}

\centering
\safeimg[width=0.88\textwidth,height=3.8cm,keepaspectratio]{cuda-graphs.pdf}

\section{Precision}
% =============================================================================

\begin{frame}{Precision Engineering}
\note{
% -- LINK: What prior concept connects to this slide
Fusion reduces the number of HBM trips. Precision engineering reduces
the bytes per trip --- a complementary optimization.

% -- NARRATE: What to SAY while showing this slide
Point to the diagram: ``Halving precision doubles effective bandwidth
--- the cheapest optimization available. FP16 to FP8 doubles throughput.
FP8 to INT4 doubles again. The challenge: LLMs have outlier features
that break uniform quantization.''

% -- ENGAGE: Specific question, prediction, or task for THIS slide
Ask: ``If you halve the precision, how much faster does a memory-bound
workload get?'' Expected: 2x, because you load half the bytes.

% -- WARN: What students will get wrong on THIS topic
Students think quantization always degrades quality. With proper
techniques (AWQ, block-wise scaling), INT4 on 70B models shows
under 1\% quality loss.

% -- FLEX: [CORE] or [OPTIONAL] + contingency
[CORE] Precision is the highest-leverage single optimization.
IF SHORT: State the principle and show only the diagram.
}

% --- Full-width diagram ---
\centering
\safeimg[width=0.92\textwidth,height=4.8cm,keepaspectratio]{precision-engineering.pdf}

\vspace{0.1cm}
\mlsysconcept{Principle:}{Fewer bytes per weight = more weights per HBM trip = higher effective bandwidth.}

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Quantization Techniques for LLMs}
\note{
% -- LINK: What prior concept connects to this slide
Precision showed the principle. This slide surveys the four main
techniques for handling LLM outlier features.

% -- NARRATE: What to SAY while showing this slide
Walk through the table: ``LLM.int8(): safe default, handles outliers
at runtime via mixed-precision decomposition. GPTQ: maximum
compression using Hessian-based calibration (hours). AWQ: production
default --- activation-aware, calibrates in minutes. SmoothQuant:
W8A8 for compute-bound regimes.'' Point to the two cards: ``PTQ first
(4 GPU-hours for 70B). QAT only if quality fails (3 days on 8xH100).
Weight-only INT4 for latency; FP8 W+A for throughput.''

% -- WARN: What students will get wrong on THIS topic
Students quantize everything to the lowest precision uniformly. LLM
outlier channels require special handling: block-wise scaling or
mixed-precision decomposition.

\begin{frame}{KV Cache Compression vs.\ Weight-Only Quantization}
\note{
% -- LINK: What prior concept connects to this slide
Weight quantization reduces model memory. But for long-context serving, the KV cache can exceed the model weights. This slide distinguishes the two quantization targets.

% -- NARRATE: What to SAY while showing this slide
``Weight-only quantization (INT4) reduces the 140 GB Llama 70B to 35 GB --- a 4x saving on static memory. But the KV cache at batch=16, seq=4K consumes 21 GB and grows linearly with batch and sequence. KV cache compression (INT8 or FP8) cuts cache memory 2x, enabling either 2x batch size or 2x sequence length. The key insight: for serving, KV cache quantization often matters more than weight quantization because it determines maximum concurrency.''

% -- ENGAGE: Specific question, prediction, or task for THIS slide
Ask: ``Which quantization target gives you more throughput?'' Expected: KV cache, because freeing KV memory allows larger batch sizes, directly increasing throughput. Weight quantization frees static memory but does not change the per-request bottleneck.

% -- WARN: What students will get wrong on THIS topic
Students apply the same quantization precision everywhere. KV cache tolerates more aggressive quantization (INT4 with minimal quality loss) because it stores intermediate activations, not learned weights.

% -- FLEX: [OPTIONAL] Distinguishes two quantization targets with different impacts.
IF SHORT: State ``KV INT8 = 2x batch size = 2x throughput'' and move on.
}

\footnotesize
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.15}
\begin{tabular}{@{}lp{2.3cm}rl@{}}
  \toprule
  \textbf{Target} & \textbf{What It Saves} & \textbf{Reduction} & \textbf{Throughput Impact} \\
  \midrule
  \textbf{Weight-Only} (INT4) & Static model memory   & 4$\times$   & Enables fitting on fewer GPUs \\
  \textbf{KV Cache} (INT8)    & Per-request memory    & 2$\times$   & 2$\times$ batch $\to$ 2$\times$ throughput \\
  \textbf{KV Cache} (INT4)    & Per-request memory    & 4$\times$   & 4$\times$ batch (with quality risk) \\
  \textbf{W+A} (FP8)          & Both weights + activations & 2$\times$ & Compute-bound speedup \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}

\vspace{0.1cm}
\begin{mlsyscard}{datastroke}

\end{mlsyscard}

\begin{frame}{Speculative Decoding}
\note{
% -- LINK: What prior concept connects to this slide
Fusion and precision reduce bytes moved. Speculative decoding increases
the useful work per byte moved --- a complementary approach.

% -- NARRATE: What to SAY while showing this slide
``LLM decode is memory-bound because each token loads the full weight
matrix from HBM for one matrix-vector multiply. Speculative decoding
uses a small draft model to predict k tokens, then the target model
verifies all k in one pass. The target's weights are loaded once for
k tokens instead of once per token. Effective AI increases by k-fold.
Speedup: 1.5--3x at batch=1.''

% -- ENGAGE: Specific question, prediction, or task for THIS slide
Ask: ``Why does speculation help at batch=1 but not at batch=64?''
Expected: at batch=64, the workload is already approaching compute-bound.
Adding speculation increases compute load without reducing bandwidth.

% -- WARN: What students will get wrong on THIS topic
Students think speculative decoding wastes compute on rejected tokens.
The insight: at batch=1, compute is abundant and bandwidth is scarce.
``Wasted'' compute costs nothing when the GPU was idle anyway.

% -- FLEX: [CORE] or [OPTIONAL] + contingency
[CORE] Speculative decoding is deployed in production systems.
IF SHORT: State the insight and the 1.5--3x result.
}

\footnotesize
\begin{columns}[T]
  \begin{column}{0.55\textwidth}
    \textbf{Problem:} Each decode step loads full weights from HBM for \emph{one} token. AI $\approx$ 2 FLOP/byte.

    \vspace{0.1cm}
    \textbf{Speculative Decoding:}
    \begin{enumerate}\setlength\itemsep{0pt}
      \item \textcolor{datastroke}{Draft model} generates $k$ candidates (fast)
      \item \textcolor{computestroke}{Target model} verifies all $k$ in \textbf{one} pass
      \item Accepted tokens: mathematically identical output
      \item Rejected tokens: ``wasted'' compute (but compute is free!)
    \end{enumerate}

    \vspace{0.05cm}
    \mlsysconcept{Trade:}{Abundant compute for scarce bandwidth.}
  \end{column}
  \begin{column}{0.42\textwidth}
    \begin{mlsyscard}{computestroke}
    {\scriptsize\textbf{Why it works:} Target loads weights \textbf{once} for $k$ tokens instead of once per token. Effective AI increases by $k\times$. Speedup: \textbf{1.5--3$\times$} typical.}
    \end{mlsyscard}

    \vspace{0.1cm}
    \begin{mlsyscard}{errorstroke}
    {\scriptsize\textbf{Caveat:} Best at batch=1 (memory-bound). At large batches, speculation adds overhead.}
    \end{mlsyscard}
  \end{column}
\end{columns}

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Fallacies}
\note{
% -- LINK: What prior concept connects to this slide
The chapter covered diagnosis (roofline), treatment (fusion, precision,
compilation, algorithms), and methodology (playbook). These fallacies
challenge remaining misconceptions.

% -- NARRATE: What to SAY while showing this slide
Read each fallacy: ``More FLOPS does not mean faster inference for
memory-bound workloads. FP8 does not halve training time for
communication-bound steps. Graph compilers cannot discover
FlashAttention-level innovations.''

% -- FLEX: [CORE] or [OPTIONAL] + contingency
[CORE] Quantitative rebuttals reinforce roofline reasoning.
IF SHORT: Cover only the first fallacy (FLOPS vs. bandwidth).
}

\small
\textbf{Fallacy:} \textit{More FLOPS means faster inference.}\\
{\footnotesize Most inference is memory-bound. A GPU with 2$\times$ FLOPS but the same bandwidth generates tokens at the same speed. Correct metric: bandwidth, not FLOPS.}

\vspace{0.15cm}
\textbf{Fallacy:} \textit{FP8 always halves training time.}\\
{\footnotesize FP8 doubles peak TFLOPS and effective BW, but only for precision-sensitive operations. Element-wise ops are launch-overhead bound. Communication-bound steps gain nothing.}

\vspace{0.15cm}
\textbf{Fallacy:} \textit{Graph compilers eliminate manual kernel engineering.}\\
{\footnotesize FlashAttention required recognizing that attention could be tiled with online softmax --- an insight beyond any compiler's rewrite rules. Compilers automate known optimizations; humans discover new ones.}

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Pitfalls}
\note{
% -- LINK: What prior concept connects to this slide
Fallacies addressed beliefs. Pitfalls address operational mistakes.

% -- NARRATE: What to SAY while showing this slide
``Optimizing the largest kernel while ignoring the long tail: when GEMM
is near-optimal, dozens of smaller ops consume over 50\% of time.
Quantizing everything uniformly: INT4 weights + INT4 KV + FP8
activations can degrade quality invisibly. Measuring throughput without
quality: 200 tok/s with 15\% quality loss is not better. Single
profiling run: thermal throttling varies 10--15\%.''

% -- FLEX: [CORE] or [OPTIONAL] + contingency
[CORE] Operational pitfalls for performance engineering.
IF SHORT: Cover only the first two pitfalls.
}

\scriptsize
\textbf{Pitfall:} \textit{Optimizing the largest kernel while ignoring the long tail.}\\
When the GEMM is near-optimal, dozens of smaller ops can consume $>$50\% of time. Graph compilation addresses this tail.

\vspace{0.08cm}
\textbf{Pitfall:} \textit{Quantizing everything to the lowest precision.}\\
INT4 weights + INT4 KV + FP8 activations can degrade quality invisibly. Targeted quantization: aggressive for KV cache, conservative for first/last layers.

\vspace{0.08cm}
\textbf{Pitfall:} \textit{Measuring throughput without measuring quality.}\\
200 tok/s with 15\% quality loss $\neq$ better than 100 tok/s at full quality. Optimize on the throughput vs.\ quality Pareto frontier.

\vspace{0.08cm}
\textbf{Pitfall:} \textit{A single profiling run characterizes performance.}\\
Thermal throttling (10--15\%), fragmentation, and congestion vary. Profile under sustained conditions.

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Key Takeaways}
\note{
% -- LINK: What prior concept connects to this slide

% -- NARRATE: What to SAY while showing this slide
Walk through each bullet with quantitative anchors: ``Ridge point 295.

% -- FLEX: [CORE] or [OPTIONAL] + contingency
[CORE] Summary after retrieval practice.
IF SHORT: Read bullets quickly.

\scriptsize
\begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{0pt}
  \item \textbf{Memory Wall}: Most transformer inference is memory-bound (AI $\ll$ 295 FLOP/byte). Reduce bytes moved, not operations computed.
  \item \textbf{Roofline Model}: Diagnose compute-bound vs.\ memory-bound \emph{before} optimizing. Wrong-term optimization yields zero improvement.
  \item \textbf{Operator Fusion}: FlashAttention reduces attention HBM traffic 65$\times$ via tiling + online softmax. CUDA Graphs eliminate launch overhead.
  \item \textbf{Precision}: INT4 (AWQ) gives 4$\times$ effective bandwidth. Block-wise quantization handles LLM outlier features.
  \item \textbf{Graph Compilation}: torch.compile (1 line, 10--40\%), XLA (55--65\% MFU), TensorRT (1.3--1.8$\times$).
  \item \textbf{Algorithmic}: Speculative decoding trades compute for latency (1.5--3$\times$). MoE decouples capacity from cost.
  \item \textbf{Profile-Optimize-Reprofile}: Every optimization shifts the bottleneck. The iterative loop is the discipline.
\end{itemize}

\end{frame}
