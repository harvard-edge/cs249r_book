\begin{abstract}
We present \staffml{}, a curated bank of \numquestions{} interview questions for ML systems engineers spanning \numtracks{} deployment tracks (cloud, edge, mobile, TinyML, global) and \numlevels{} mastery levels mapped to Bloom's revised taxonomy. Every question is classified on four independent axes: \emph{topic} (\numtopics{} curated concepts organized into \numareas{} competency areas with a prerequisite graph of \numedges{} edges), \emph{zone} (\numzones{} ikigai-inspired cognitive zones that capture \emph{how} a topic is tested), \emph{track} (which physics regime the question targets), and \emph{level} (what cognitive depth is required). Unlike existing interview preparation resources that focus on algorithmic coding, \staffml{} tests \emph{mechanical sympathy} --- the ability to reason quantitatively about the physical constraints of ML infrastructure. Every question requires napkin math grounded in real hardware specifications: memory bandwidth limits, thermal envelopes, interconnect topologies, and the economics of accelerator fleets. The key intellectual contribution is the \emph{ikigai competency model}, where four fundamental skills (recall, analyze, design, quantify\footnote{We use ``quantify'' rather than ``implement'' to emphasize that this skill tests the ability to produce concrete numbers from specifications, not the ability to write production code.}) combine pairwise into six compound cognitive zones (diagnosis, specification, fluency, evaluation, realization, optimization) and unite into a mastery zone. This model replaces traditional flat taxonomies with a structured vocabulary for what it means to ``know'' ML systems engineering. The classification schema is defined in LinkML and generates Pydantic models, JSON Schema, and TypeScript types from a single source of truth. Quality is enforced by a \numinvariants{}-check invariant system, multi-model math verification, and three-stage deduplication.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The role of the ML systems engineer has emerged as one of the most sought-after positions in the technology industry. Unlike ML researchers who design model architectures or software engineers who write application code, ML systems engineers must reason about the \emph{physical constraints} of deploying machine learning at scale: memory bandwidth limits that determine whether a workload is compute-bound or memory-bound, thermal envelopes that cap sustained throughput on edge devices, network congestion that dominates training time in distributed clusters, and the economics of accelerator fleets where a single misconfigured batch size can waste thousands of dollars per hour.

This is what Martin Thompson calls \emph{mechanical sympathy} \citep{thompson2011sympathy} --- a term borrowed from racing driver Jackie Stewart's philosophy that a driver performs best when they understand what the car is doing underneath them. A Formula~1 driver who understands the engine does not merely drive faster; they drive \emph{differently}, making decisions that are physically impossible to arrive at through pure algorithmic thinking. Similarly, an ML systems engineer who understands that autoregressive decode reads the full 140\,GB model weights for every single token --- achieving an arithmetic intensity of just 1\,FLOP/Byte, far below the hardware's 148\,FLOP/Byte ridge point --- does not merely optimize code; they architect systems that batch requests to amortize the bandwidth cost.

The growing field of AI engineering demands practitioners who can reason about safety, efficiency, robustness, and reliability --- not just model accuracy. \staffml{} assesses these systems-level competencies that distinguish engineers who can deploy ML responsibly at scale.

In an era where large language models can generate code, write documentation, and pass coding interviews, one might ask: why do ML systems engineers still need to reason from first principles? The answer is that \emph{you cannot prompt your way out of a memory wall}. No amount of LLM-assisted coding will tell you that your serving cluster is memory-bandwidth-bound at an arithmetic intensity of 1\,FLOP/Byte, or that your KV-cache will consume 42\,GB at 128K context, or that thermal throttling will cut your edge device's sustained throughput by 40\%. These are physics problems, and physics requires quantitative reasoning grounded in real hardware specifications.

We use the term \emph{physics-grounded} to mean that every question has a numerical answer derivable from real hardware specifications: memory bandwidths, thermal envelopes, interconnect latencies, and power budgets. The companion MLSysIM simulator \citep{reddi2026mlsysim} provides the shared physics engine from which all hardware constants are drawn, ensuring that questions are numerically consistent and verifiable. A candidate who internalizes these physical constants can derive answers for any model on any hardware, rather than memorizing configuration recipes that expire with each hardware generation.

Yet interview preparation resources for this role are remarkably thin. LeetCode \citep{leetcode2024} offers 2,800+ algorithmic coding problems but nothing on GPU memory hierarchies or KV-cache sizing. Huyen's \emph{Designing Machine Learning Systems} \citep{huyen2022designing} provides excellent conceptual coverage but no interactive, quantitative practice (see \Cref{sec:related} for a detailed comparison). Mock interview platforms focus on behavioral and coding rounds, not system design with napkin math.\footnote{\emph{Napkin math} (back-of-envelope calculation) is rapid quantitative estimation from first principles. For example: ``How much VRAM does Llama-70B's KV-cache need at 128K context?'' $\to$ $2 \times 80 \times 8 \times 128 \times 128\text{K} \times 2 \approx 42\text{\,GB}$. The candidate derives the answer in real time from five numbers: layers, KV-heads, head dimension, sequence length, and bytes per element.} The result is a preparation gap: candidates study the wrong material, and interviewers lack a validated question bank calibrated to the specific cognitive demands of systems reasoning.

\begin{quote}
\footnotesize
\textbf{Example (diagnosis zone, cloud track, L4):} Your production LLM serving cluster reports 40\% MFU during the decode phase. The H100s are rated at 495\,TFLOPS FP16 dense. Your team assumes the GPUs are underclocked. Diagnose the actual root cause.

\emph{Common mistake:} Blaming hardware or driver issues.

\emph{Napkin math:} (1)~Decode reads the full model weights per token: $2 \times 70\text{B} = 140\,\text{GB}$ in FP16. (2)~Compute per token: $2 \times 70\text{B} = 140\,\text{GFLOPs}$. (3)~Arithmetic intensity $= 140\,\text{GFLOPs} \div 140\,\text{GB} = 1\,\text{FLOP/Byte}$. (4)~H100 ridge point $= 495\,\text{TFLOPS} \div 3.35\,\text{TB/s} \approx 148\,\text{FLOPs/Byte}$.

\emph{Answer:} The decode phase is memory-bandwidth-bound. At arithmetic intensity of 1\,FLOP/Byte, the workload sits far below the 148\,FLOP/Byte ridge point. MFU measures compute utilization, but the bottleneck is data movement. The 40\% figure is not a deficiency --- the GPUs are performing as expected.
\end{quote}

The intellectual foundation of \staffml{} is the thesis that \textbf{constraints drive architecture}. You do not choose a Transformer because it is trendy; you choose it because its attention mechanism parallelizes across thousands of cores on real silicon, unlike the sequential dependency chains of RNNs. You do not use mixed precision because a tutorial told you to; you use it because FP16 tensor cores deliver $2\times$ the throughput of FP32 and halve the memory footprint, changing whether your model fits on one GPU or requires four. This philosophy, drawn from the \emph{Machine Learning Systems} textbook \citep{reddi2026mlsys}, insists that AI is not magic; it is infrastructure, and infrastructure has laws.

The constraints that drive ML systems architecture can be distilled into five fundamental laws:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item \textbf{The Roofline Bound.} Every workload is either compute-bound or memory-bandwidth-bound, determined by its arithmetic intensity relative to the hardware's ridge point.
\item \textbf{The Memory Capacity Wall.} Model weights, optimizer state, activations, and KV-cache must fit simultaneously in available memory; exceeding capacity forces parallelism, offloading, or compression.
\item \textbf{The Communication Tax.} In distributed systems, gradient synchronization, tensor transfers, and pipeline bubbles consume time proportional to model size and inversely proportional to interconnect bandwidth.
\item \textbf{The Power Envelope.} Sustained throughput is bounded by thermal dissipation, not peak compute; real performance is the minimum of compute capability and cooling capacity.
\item \textbf{The Cost Multiplier.} Every architecture decision compounds through fleet size, electricity cost, and amortization period; a 2$\times$ efficiency improvement at 1,000 GPUs saves millions of dollars annually.
\end{enumerate}

Every \staffml{} question ultimately tests reasoning about one or more of these laws. The topic taxonomy maps concepts to the laws they exercise; the zone axis determines how that reasoning is assessed.

\staffml{} fills this gap with thousands of questions that test mechanical sympathy across the full ML deployment spectrum. Every question has a realistic interview scenario drawn from industry practice, a common mistake that candidates typically make, a realistic solution with quantitative reasoning, napkin math grounded in real hardware specifications, and classification by topic (what concept), zone (how it is tested), track (where it deploys), and level (how hard it is). \staffml{} is the companion assessment tool for the \emph{Machine Learning Systems} textbook \citep{reddi2026mlsys}, developed alongside the Harvard CS249r course \citep{cs249r}.

\paragraph{Three practical workflows.}

\staffml{} supports three practical workflows:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item \textbf{Interview preparation.} Candidates filter questions by target role level (L4--L6+), practice zone, and deployment track. Depth chains provide structured study paths from fundamentals to synthesis (see \Cref{sec:chains} for depth chains that structure study paths).
\item \textbf{Interview design.} Hiring managers select questions across zones to construct balanced interview loops. The zone model provides a shared vocabulary for debrief --- ``strong in diagnosis, weak in specification'' (\Cref{sec:practical} describes interview loop design).
\item \textbf{Curriculum alignment.} Educators map course content to the topic taxonomy, identifying which competency cells their curriculum covers and where gaps remain (the prerequisite graph in \Cref{sec:topics} suggests teaching order).
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Backward design.}

The paper follows a \emph{backward design} methodology \citep{wiggins2005understanding}: we start from the desired result (what a Staff engineer must demonstrate), derive the assessment structure that would produce evidence of those competencies, and only then construct the questions themselves. The corpus size is an \emph{output} of this derivation, not an input.

The backward design chain proceeds in five steps, and each step corresponds to a section of this paper:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{What must they demonstrate?} We decompose Staff-level mechanical sympathy into 4 atomic skills (recall, analyze, design, quantify) whose pairwise combinations yield \numzones{} cognitive zones (\Cref{sec:competency-model}).
  \item \textbf{What do we test on?} We identify \numtopics{} curated topics spanning \numareas{} competency areas as the minimum spanning set of ML systems knowledge (\Cref{sec:topics}).
  \item \textbf{Where is it meaningful?} Not every concept has a physical substrate on every hardware tier. We derive an applicability matrix across 4 deployment tracks, excluding topic--track pairs with physics-grounded justifications (\Cref{sec:tracks}, \Cref{sec:coverage}).
  \item \textbf{How many distinct questions exist?} We empirically bound the number of meaningfully distinct questions per cell through capacity analysis (\Cref{sec:coverage}).
  \item \textbf{Is it correct?} Quality infrastructure with \numinvariants{} invariants, multi-model math verification, and three-stage deduplication ensures every question is physics-correct (\Cref{sec:qa}).
\end{enumerate}

The intersection of these five constraints derives a principled corpus where every question traces back to ``what must a Staff ML systems engineer demonstrate?'' \Cref{sec:examples} presents example questions. \Cref{sec:practical} discusses practical interview design. \Cref{sec:related} surveys related work. \Cref{sec:limitations} discusses limitations and future work.

\section{Methodology: Backward Design}
\label{sec:methodology}

The structure of \staffml{} is not an organizational convenience; it is a derivation. We follow the \emph{backward design} methodology of Wiggins and McTighe~\citep{wiggins2005understanding}, which proceeds in three stages: identify the desired results, determine what constitutes acceptable evidence, and then---only then---design the assessment. The corpus size is an \emph{output} of this derivation, not an input. We did not begin with a target of $N$ questions and fill to quota; we defined the competency space, filtered it by physics, bounded its capacity empirically, and let the total fall out.

\Cref{fig:backward-design} illustrates the five-step derivation chain. Each step constrains the next, and the final corpus is fully determined by the intersection of those constraints.

\begin{figure}[t!]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{fig-backward-design.svg}
\caption{\textbf{The backward design derivation chain.} Corpus size is an output of principled constraints, not an input. Each step narrows the assessment space: competency decomposition yields 12 zones, topic curation yields 86 concepts, the physics filter excludes impossible hardware pairings, and capacity analysis bounds meaningful question density.}
\label{fig:backward-design}
\end{figure}

\subsection{The Derivation Chain}

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, label=\textbf{Step \arabic*.}]

\item \textbf{Decompose competency into skills and zones.}
We begin from the desired result: \emph{what must a Staff-level ML systems engineer demonstrate?} The answer is mechanical sympathy---the ability to connect hardware constraints to architectural decisions using quantitative reasoning. We decompose this into four atomic skills (recall, analyze, design, quantify) and observe that real engineering tasks never exercise one skill in isolation. A diagnosis requires recall \emph{and} analysis; napkin math requires recall \emph{and} quantification. The pairwise combinations of four skills yield six compound zones, plus one mastery zone that exercises all four. This produces the \numzones{} cognitive zones described in \Cref{sec:competency-model}.

The zone model departs from Bloom's revised taxonomy~\citep{anderson2001taxonomy}, which arranges cognitive operations in a hierarchy (remember $\to$ create), implying each level subsumes the previous. This hierarchical structure cannot represent competencies that \emph{combine} skills laterally. Consider fluency (recall $\cap$ quantify): an engineer who retrieves hardware specifications from memory \emph{and} executes napkin math in real time. Bloom has no single level for this act. The ikigai model treats skills as \emph{dimensions}, not \emph{levels}. The zones are the cells of a combinatorial lattice: $\binom{4}{1} + \binom{4}{2} + 1 = 11$.

\item \textbf{Define topic coverage.}
We identify \numtopics{} topics spanning \numareas{} competency areas as the minimum spanning set of Staff-level ML systems knowledge. The prerequisite graph (\numedges{} edges) encodes dependencies.

\item \textbf{Apply the physics filter.}
Not every topic has a physical substrate on every hardware tier. We derive an \emph{applicability matrix} that excludes \numexcludedpairs{} topic--track pairs with one-sentence physics justifications. The remaining \numapplicablepairs{} pairs define the valid assessment space. This establishes \emph{content validity}~\citep{messick1995validity}: every question references a concept with physical meaning on the targeted hardware tier.

\item \textbf{Bound capacity empirically.}
For each valid cell, how many meaningfully distinct questions exist? Capacity varies by cognitive complexity and difficulty level. These bounds are hypotheses validated through expert enumeration and planned saturation studies.

\item \textbf{Validate through convergence.}
We track whether expert reviewers identify new structural gaps across rounds. When feedback shifts from ``missing X'' to ``improve phrasing of Y,'' the framework is structurally converged. Twelve domain experts reviewed the framework; the structure stabilized after corrections to the applicability matrix and addition of consensus topics.

\end{enumerate}

Construct validity---whether zones capture psychometrically distinct competencies---requires inter-rater reliability data (Cohen's $\kappa > 0.7$) and Item Response Theory~\citep{lord1980irt} calibration from candidate response data. Both are planned as future work (\Cref{sec:limitations}).

\section{The StaffML Competency Model}
\label{sec:competency-model}

What makes ML systems expertise different from algorithmic programming skill? A software engineer who can implement a Transformer from scratch \citep{reddi2026tinytorch} may still be unable to answer a deceptively simple question: ``How much VRAM does that model need, and why does that number determine your entire serving architecture?'' The gap is not knowledge of algorithms; it is \emph{mechanical sympathy} --- the ability to reason quantitatively about how software interacts with physical hardware.

The TinyTorch competency matrix \citep{reddi2026tinytorch} defines \emph{what to teach} through 8 knowledge areas crossed with 5 capability levels; the \staffml{} ikigai model defines \emph{how to assess} whether those competencies have been acquired.

\staffml{} begins from the observation that ML systems expertise is not a single skill but the \emph{intersection} of multiple cognitive modes. An engineer who can recall every hardware specification but cannot analyze a failure is incomplete. An engineer who designs elegant architectures but cannot produce napkin math to validate them is dangerous. The competency model must capture these intersections explicitly.

\subsection{Four Fundamental Skills}

We decompose ML systems expertise into four fundamental skills. The model is inspired by the Japanese concept of \emph{ikigai}\footnote{\emph{Ikigai} is a Japanese concept describing the intersection of passion, skill, need, and reward. We adapt its four-circle Venn diagram structure to model intersecting cognitive skills in ML systems engineering.} --- the intersection of overlapping life domains --- adapted here as four skill domains whose intersections define qualitatively different cognitive acts.

Every act of ML systems reasoning exercises one or more of these skills:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Recall.} Facts, definitions, hardware specifications. ``What is the HBM bandwidth of an H100?'' The engineer retrieves a stored constant.
  \item \textbf{Analyze.} Tradeoffs, reasoning, root cause analysis. ``Why is this workload memory-bound?'' The engineer reasons about the interaction between compute and data movement.
  \item \textbf{Design.} Architecture decisions, requirements-to-system mapping. ``Design a serving stack with P99 $<$ 100\,ms.'' The engineer translates constraints into an architecture.
  \item \textbf{Quantify.} Napkin math \& estimation. ``Calculate the VRAM required for a 70B model at 128K context.'' The engineer produces a specific number from a formula and hardware specs.\footnote{We use ``quantify'' rather than ``implement'' to emphasize that this skill tests the ability to produce concrete numbers from specifications, not the ability to write production code. The distinction matters: quantification is a reasoning act (estimating, bounding, computing), whereas implementation implies building artifacts.}
\end{enumerate}

These four skills are not Bloom's levels; they are orthogonal dimensions of expertise. A senior engineer may have deep recall and strong quantification skills (fluency) but weak design ability; another may excel at analysis and design (evaluation) but struggle with napkin math. The ikigai model makes these profiles visible and assessable.

\subsection{Eleven Cognitive Zones}

The four skills combine into 11 zones: 4 pure zones (exercising a single skill), 6 compound zones (exercising two skills simultaneously), and 1 mastery zone (all four). \Cref{fig:competency-model} illustrates the model as a four-ellipse Venn diagram. We deliberately omit the four possible three-skill intersections (e.g., recall $\cap$ analyze $\cap$ design) because, in practice, questions exercising three skills almost always exercise all four, making them mastery questions. The two-skill compound zones represent the cognitively distinctive acts; adding three-skill zones would dilute rather than sharpen the classification.

\begin{figure*}[t!]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.80\textwidth]{fig-competency-model.pdf}
\caption{\textbf{The \staffml{} ikigai competency model.} Four fundamental skills (Recall, Analyze, Design, Quantify) overlap to produce six compound zones and one central mastery zone. Each question targets exactly one zone, which determines \emph{how} a topic is assessed. The compound zones capture the cognitive acts that distinguish Staff-level engineers: diagnosing failures (Recall $\cap$ Analyze), translating specifications into architectures (Recall $\cap$ Design), performing napkin math from memory (Recall $\cap$ Quantify), evaluating architectural alternatives (Analyze $\cap$ Design), sizing concrete systems (Design $\cap$ Quantify), and optimizing bottlenecks (Analyze $\cap$ Quantify).}
\label{fig:competency-model}
\end{figure*}

\textbf{Pure zones} test a single skill in isolation:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Recall.} ``What is the ridge point of an H100?'' Retrieve a fact.
  \item \textbf{Analyze.} ``Why does GPU utilization collapse at batch size 1?'' Reason about system behavior.
  \item \textbf{Design.} ``Architect a KV-cache paging system.'' Produce an architecture.
  \item \textbf{Quantify.} ``Write the formula for KV-cache memory.'' Produce a concrete artifact.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Compound zones} test two skills simultaneously. These are the zones that most sharply differentiate strong from weak candidates, because they require integrating knowledge across cognitive modes:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Diagnosis} (Recall $\cap$ Analyze). ``MFU is 40\%, diagnose it.'' The candidate must recall hardware specifications \emph{and} reason about which bottleneck explains the gap. This is the ``3\,AM on-call'' skill.
  \item \textbf{Specification} (Recall $\cap$ Design). ``P99 $<$ 100\,ms with 70B model, design it.'' The candidate must recall hardware capabilities \emph{and} translate the latency constraint into an architecture (tensor parallelism degree, batch size, KV-cache budget).
  \item \textbf{Fluency} (Recall $\cap$ Quantify). ``How much VRAM does a 70B model need?'' The candidate must recall the formula and hardware specs \emph{and} execute the arithmetic. This is napkin math from memory, the signature skill of Staff+ engineers.
  \item \textbf{Evaluation} (Analyze $\cap$ Design). ``MoE vs.\ dense: which architecture for this serving workload?'' The candidate must analyze the tradeoffs \emph{and} reason about which design better satisfies the constraints. No recall is required; the specs are given.
  \item \textbf{Realization} (Design $\cap$ Quantify). ``Size the micro-batches for this pipeline-parallel training job.'' The candidate must design the pipeline schedule \emph{and} produce concrete numbers (batch splits, memory per stage, bubble fraction).
  \item \textbf{Optimization} (Analyze $\cap$ Quantify). ``Fix the memory wall.'' The candidate must analyze the bottleneck \emph{and} produce a concrete fix (kernel fusion, quantization, offloading) with measurable improvement.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Mastery} (all four skills) represents full Staff-level synthesis: ``Your LLM serving cluster's TTFT spiked from 100\,ms to 4\,s after a traffic surge. Diagnose the root cause, design a fix, size the required resources, and estimate the cost.'' This zone requires recalling hardware specs, analyzing the failure mode, designing an architectural solution, and executing the napkin math to validate it.

This affinity is enforced as a soft constraint during question generation. An L1 question tagged as ``evaluation'' is flagged for review, since evaluation (analyze $\cap$ design) is cognitively inconsistent with Bloom's ``Remember'' level.

\subsection{The Four-Axis Classification}

The competency model answers \emph{what cognitive skills matter}. To operationalize this into a practical assessment framework, \staffml{} classifies every question on four independent axes, each capturing a distinct dimension of ML systems expertise. \Cref{fig:four-axes} illustrates the architecture.

\begin{figure}[t!]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{fig-four-axes.pdf}
\caption{\textbf{Four-axis classification.} Every question is independently classified by \emph{topic} (what concept is tested), \emph{zone} (how the concept is tested), \emph{track} (which physics regime applies), and \emph{level} (what cognitive depth is required). The axes are orthogonal: any valid combination defines a unique assessment cell.}
\label{fig:four-axes}
\end{figure}

The \textbf{topic axis} identifies \emph{what} concept a question tests. The \numtopics{} topics are organized into \numareas{} competency areas, from memory hierarchies and parallelism strategies to power budgeting and reliability, and connected by \numedges{} typed edges (prerequisite, broader, narrower, related) that encode the dependency structure of ML systems knowledge.

The \textbf{zone axis} captures \emph{how} a concept is tested, using the \numzones{} ikigai zones defined above. The zone determines the cognitive profile of a question independently of its subject matter.

The \textbf{track axis} specifies \emph{where} the scenario takes place --- which physics regime governs the problem. Cloud questions operate at PFLOPS scale under memory bandwidth and network fabric constraints. Edge questions operate under thermal envelopes and real-time deadlines. Mobile questions face battery budgets and shared-resource contention. TinyML questions confront SRAM limits and hard real-time on bare metal. A fifth ``global'' track captures cross-cutting principles.

The \textbf{level axis} encodes \emph{how hard} the question is, mapped to Bloom's revised taxonomy \citep{anderson2001taxonomy}: L1 (Remember) through L6+ (Create). The levels also correspond to industry engineering ladders from intern through Staff+.

These four axes are orthogonal by design. A question about KV-cache management (topic) can test fluency (zone) in the cloud regime (track) at L3 difficulty (level), or it can test diagnosis in the edge regime at L4. The three-dimensional coverage space of topic $\times$ zone $\times$ track contains $79 \times 11 \times 5 = 4{,}345$ cells. The level dimension is not a separate coverage axis; instead, it is captured by the zone--level affinity (\Cref{tab:zone-levels}), which determines the natural difficulty range for each zone. Restricting to valid combinations (each topic applies to a subset of tracks) yields approximately 4{,}455 meaningful cells, of which 92\% are currently populated. The combinatorial structure enables precise gap analysis --- the coverage space reveals exactly which assessment cells are populated, underfilled, or empty.

\subsection{Depth Chains}
\label{sec:chains}

Questions on the same topic are linked into \emph{depth chains} that progress through Bloom's levels, testing increasingly sophisticated cognitive acts. A chain ensures that assessment covers the full range from recall to synthesis. The corpus contains \numchains{} chains spanning three or more levels.

For example, a chain on KV-cache management progresses through six levels and six zones:

\begin{quote}
\footnotesize
\textbf{L1 (Recall):} ``What is a KV-cache and why does it grow with sequence length?'' \\
\textbf{L2 (Quantify):} ``Calculate the KV-cache size for Llama-70B at 128K context.'' \\
\textbf{L3 (Fluency):} ``Your 80\,GB GPU OOMs at 128K context. Derive the memory breakdown.'' \\
\textbf{L4 (Diagnosis):} ``PagedAttention reduces memory by 60\%. Explain the mechanism.'' \\
\textbf{L5 (Specification):} ``Design a KV-cache compression strategy. What precision trade-offs are acceptable?'' \\
\textbf{L6+ (Mastery):} ``Your paged KV-cache suffers fragmentation under variable-length batching. Diagnose, design a fix, and size the memory overhead.''
\end{quote}

Each step in the chain builds on the previous, and the zone shifts from recall through quantification to diagnosis and finally mastery. Chains serve three purposes: (1) validating that the topic taxonomy supports questions at multiple cognitive levels, (2) enabling progressive study paths where candidates build from fundamentals to synthesis, and (3) providing interviewers with calibrated question sequences that reveal the candidate's ceiling.

The corpus contains \numchains{} topic clusters spanning 3+ levels, with \numfullchains{} full chains spanning all six levels from L1 to L6+. Of the \numchains{} chains, 715 span 3 levels, 104 span 4 levels, 83 span 5 levels, and 109 span all 6 levels. In total, 3{,}417 questions (64.4\% of the corpus) participate in at least one chain.

\subsection{The Prerequisite Graph}

Topics are connected by \numedges{} typed edges following the SKOS vocabulary \citep{skos2009}:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Prerequisite} (57 edges). Topic A must be understood before Topic B. Example: \texttt{gpu-compute-architecture} requires \texttt{roofline-analysis} as a prerequisite, because diagnosing whether a GPU kernel is compute-bound or memory-bound requires the roofline model. The graph is verified to be acyclic with a maximum prerequisite depth of 3.
  \item \textbf{Broader/Narrower} (14 edges). Hierarchical containment. Example: \texttt{parallelism-strategies} is broader than \texttt{tensor-parallelism}.
  \item \textbf{Related} (52 edges). Lateral connection. Example: \texttt{roofline-analysis} is related to \texttt{profiling-bottleneck-analysis}.
\end{itemize}

The prerequisite edges enable automated learning path generation. Given a target topic, the system computes the transitive closure of prerequisites and produces an ordered study sequence. The graph has 31 root topics (concepts with no prerequisites) and a maximum prerequisite depth of 3, ensuring that no learning path requires more than three preparatory topics.

At each node, the candidate starts with L1--L2 recall and fluency questions to build foundational knowledge, then progresses to L3--L4 diagnosis and evaluation questions before moving to the next topic. The full path from roofline fundamentals to 3D parallelism mastery spans approximately 60 questions across 20 depth chains.

The current maximum prerequisite depth of 3 reflects the initial curation scope. Real ML systems knowledge has deeper dependency chains. For example, understanding why a specific collective communication pattern matters requires understanding network topology, bandwidth-latency tradeoffs, and the roofline model --- a chain of depth 4 or more. Deepening the prerequisite graph is planned for future taxonomy revisions, as more topics cross the inclusion threshold and longer chains become expressible.

\subsection{Knowledge Organization}

The taxonomy follows the principles of faceted classification \citep{hjorland2013facet}, where each question is independently classified on orthogonal axes rather than slotted into a single hierarchy. This design avoids the cross-classification problems inherent in monohierarchical schemes. A question about KV-cache memory pressure during continuous batching touches memory, latency, and deployment simultaneously, and a faceted system accommodates this naturally.

Topic selection follows user warrant \citep{soergel1985organizing} rather than literary warrant. Topics are included because engineers need them during interviews, not because they appear frequently in the research literature. This distinction explains why the taxonomy includes \texttt{tco-cost-modeling} (rarely a research topic, always an interview topic) but excludes attention mechanism variants (heavily researched, rarely asked as systems questions).

The taxonomy evolves through continuous monitoring of systems research: new topics are proposed based on industry job postings, conference proceedings (MLSys, OSDI, SOSP), and interview feedback. Each candidate topic must pass the three inclusion filters before formal inclusion; topics that fail the reasoning-depth filter are deferred until sufficient multi-level questions can be written.

\label{sec:tracks}

\subsection{Cloud: Bandwidth and Fabric}

In the cloud regime, compute is abundant but data movement is expensive. An H100 GPU delivers 989\,TFLOPS of FP16 compute with 2:4 structured sparsity (495\,TFLOPS dense)\footnote{NVIDIA's headline 989\,TFLOPS figure assumes 2:4 structured sparsity, which doubles effective throughput for compatible workloads. Most LLM inference and training workloads do not use structured sparsity, so we use the dense figure of 495\,TFLOPS for napkin math throughout this paper unless otherwise noted.} but only 3.35\,TB/s of HBM bandwidth, yielding a ridge point of $495 / 3.35 \approx 148$\,FLOPs/Byte for dense workloads. Most LLM inference workloads, particularly the autoregressive decode phase, operate well below this ridge point, making them memory-bandwidth-bound despite the massive compute available. The dominant engineering challenges are KV-cache management, continuous batching \citep{yu2022orca} to maximize GPU utilization, and inter-node communication for distributed training where InfiniBand fabric at 400\,Gb/s still becomes the bottleneck during AllReduce operations.

Cloud questions test reasoning about throughput, latency, and cost at datacenter scale. Napkin math involves TFLOPS, TB/s, and dollars per GPU-hour.

\subsection{Edge: Thermal and Real-Time}

Edge deployment (autonomous vehicles, robotics, industrial inspection) operates under a thermal envelope that caps sustained throughput. An NVIDIA Orin delivers 275\,TOPS INT8 within a 60\,W power budget, but sustained operation at maximum throughput may require thermal throttling. The dominant constraint is the intersection of compute budget and real-time deadlines. The perception pipeline must process a camera frame within 33\,ms (30\,fps) regardless of scene complexity, leaving no room for the tail-latency spikes that cloud systems tolerate.

Edge questions test reasoning about worst-case execution time, power budgets, and the tradeoff between model accuracy and inference latency. Napkin math involves TOPS, Watts, and milliseconds.

\subsection{Mobile: Battery and Resources}

Mobile ML operates on shared silicon --- the neural processing unit (NPU) shares power, thermal headroom, and memory bandwidth with the CPU, GPU, camera ISP, and cellular modem. A smartphone's 4,500\,mAh battery must last all day, meaning any ML workload must justify its energy cost against competing demands. The dominant constraint is TOPS per Watt. A mobile NPU may deliver 15\,TOPS at 5\,W, but running it continuously for on-device inference would drain the battery in under 2 hours.

Mobile questions test reasoning about energy budgets, model compression for on-device deployment, and the scheduling tradeoffs of running ML inference alongside other system tasks.

\subsection{TinyML: SRAM and Real-Time}

TinyML represents the most constrained physics regime. A Cortex-M4 microcontroller operates at $\sim$100\,MFLOPS with 256\,KB of SRAM and no operating system \citep{lin2020mcunet}. There is no virtual memory, no swap space, and no dynamic allocation; the model, activations, and runtime must all fit in SRAM simultaneously. The dominant constraint is hard real-time. A keyword spotting model must classify an audio frame within a fixed time budget, because missing a deadline means dropping audio samples with no recovery mechanism.

TinyML questions test reasoning about model footprint in kilobytes, fixed-point arithmetic, and the systems engineering required to run inference on bare metal. Napkin math involves MFLOPS, KB, and microseconds.

\subsection{Cross-Track Variation}

The track structure ensures that questions test regime-appropriate reasoning. Consider KV-cache management across tracks:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Cloud.} ``A 70B model's KV-cache at 128K context is $\sim$42\,GB. How many H100s do you need?'' (Memory capacity reasoning at TB scale)
  \item \textbf{Edge.} ``Your ADAS model caches 10 frames of detection history. At 30\,fps on Orin's 32\,GB LPDDR5, what is the memory pressure?'' (Real-time memory budget reasoning)
  \item \textbf{TinyML.} ``Your keyword spotter needs 2\,KB of rolling buffer on a 256\,KB MCU. What fraction of SRAM does this consume, and what is left for the model?'' (SRAM partitioning at KB scale)
\end{itemize}

All three questions test the same underlying concept (caching intermediate computation to avoid recomputation) but the physics, the formulas, and the failure modes are entirely different.

Each level tests a qualitatively different cognitive operation. L1 requires memorization of physical constants (``HBM3 latency is $\sim$300\,ns''). L3 requires applying a formula to a novel scenario (``Calculate KV-cache size for Llama-70B at 128K context''). L6+ requires synthesizing novel architectures from first principles (``Design a scheduling policy that prevents prefill starvation under continuous batching'').

The industry ladder mapping is intentional, not incidental. When a hiring manager at Google prepares an L5 (Senior) interview loop, they can filter \staffml{} for L4--L5 questions and know that the cognitive demands match the leveling expectations. When a candidate preparing for a Staff (L6) role wants to practice, they can target L5--L6+ questions that require the systems-level synthesis expected at that level. This alignment between Bloom's cognitive framework and industry practice is what makes the level axis actionable for both sides of the interview table.

We adopt Bloom's revised taxonomy \citep{anderson2001taxonomy} over alternatives such as Webb's Depth of Knowledge \citep{webb1997dok} and the SOLO taxonomy \citep{biggs1982solo} for three reasons. First, Bloom's six levels align naturally with the six tiers of industry engineering ladders, providing an intuitive mapping that interviewers and candidates already understand. Second, Bloom's cognitive verbs (remember, understand, apply, analyze, evaluate, create) map directly to the cognitive operations demanded by systems interview questions, making level assignment more reliable than the content-area-dependent DOK levels. Third, the revised taxonomy's two-dimensional structure (cognitive process $\times$ knowledge type) complements rather than conflicts with the orthogonal zone axis. Bloom's captures \emph{depth}, while zones capture \emph{mode}.

The level distribution in the corpus reflects intentional design decisions. L3--L4 questions (Apply and Analyze) are the most numerous because they correspond to the cognitive sweet spot of technical interviews --- complex enough to differentiate candidates, but tractable within a 45-minute interview slot. L1--L2 questions serve as warm-up and screening, while L6+ questions are reserved for Staff+ interview loops where candidates are expected to synthesize novel solutions.

\section{Schema and Infrastructure}
\label{sec:infrastructure}

The \staffml{} infrastructure translates the conceptual design (four axes, \numzones{} zones, \numtopics{} topics) into a concrete, validated data system. The design follows a ``define once, generate everywhere'' principle. A single LinkML schema serves as the source of truth, generating Pydantic models for Python runtime validation, JSON Schema for CI/CD pipelines, and TypeScript types for downstream applications. This eliminates the class of bugs where one layer accepts a value that another rejects.

The schema defines the \texttt{Topic} class (id, name, area, description, tracks, edges), the \texttt{Edge} class (target, edge\_type, note), and all valid enumerations (\numzones{} zones, \numareas{} areas, \numtracks{} tracks, \numlevels{} levels). Adding a new topic or zone requires editing the LinkML schema first; all downstream artifacts regenerate automatically.

\begin{lstlisting}[caption={\textbf{LinkML schema excerpt.} The Topic class definition generates Pydantic, JSON Schema, and TypeScript from a single YAML source.}, label={lst:schema}, morekeywords={classes,attributes,class_uri,identifier,pattern,slot_uri,required,range,multivalued}]
classes:
  Topic:
    class_uri: skos:Concept
    attributes:
      id:
        identifier: true
        pattern: "^[a-z0-9]+(-[a-z0-9]+)*$"
      name:
        slot_uri: skos:prefLabel
        required: true
      area:
        range: CompetencyArea
        required: true
      edges:
        range: TopicEdge
        multivalued: true
\end{lstlisting}

The prerequisite graph encoded in the schema is verified to be a directed acyclic graph (DAG), ensuring that learning paths are well-ordered. Structural consistency across the corpus is enforced by an invariant checking system that runs as a CI gate --- no commit is accepted if any invariant fails. These invariants catch errors that no single-question validator can detect, such as a topic deleted from the taxonomy but still referenced by questions, a prerequisite cycle introduced by a new edge, or a topic that has accumulated an outsized share of questions. The full invariant system is described in \Cref{sec:qa}.

\section{Item Construction}
\label{sec:generation}

Every \staffml{} question is constructed to ensure quantitative grounding and cognitive alignment with the competency model. The methodology draws on Evidence-Centered Design (ECD) \citep{mislevy2003ecd} and Automatic Item Generation (AIG) \citep{gierl2013aig}, prioritizing the structural properties that make questions valid assessments of ML systems expertise.

\subsection{Evidence-Centered Design}

Every question begins with a specification of three ECD components: (1) the \emph{competency claim} --- what the question measures, defined by its topic $\times$ zone $\times$ track $\times$ level coordinates; (2) the \emph{evidence} --- what a correct answer demonstrates, mapped to Bloom's cognitive operation; and (3) the \emph{task features} --- the scenario elements that elicit that evidence (hardware specs, failure mode, scale). The four-axis classification is specified \emph{before} question construction, ensuring coverage is intentional rather than emergent.

\subsection{Hardware Grounding}

\begin{figure}[t!]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{fig-generation-pipeline.pdf}
\caption{\textbf{Item construction pipeline.} Gap analysis identifies underfilled cells in the coverage space. Questions are constructed with hardware constants, Bloom's descriptors, zone descriptions, and track constraints. Candidates pass through schema validation, multi-stage deduplication, and multi-model math verification before corpus integration.}
\label{fig:generation-pipeline}
\end{figure}

Each question is grounded in five components drawn from the schema:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Topic description} from the taxonomy --- what concept to test
  \item \textbf{Zone description} --- how to test it (which skills to exercise)
  \item \textbf{Track constraints} --- which physics regime applies
  \item \textbf{Level expectations} --- which Bloom's cognitive operation to target
  \item \textbf{Hardware constants} --- real specifications (H100: 80\,GB HBM3, 3.35\,TB/s, 495\,TFLOPS FP16 dense; Orin: 275\,TOPS INT8; Cortex-M4: $\sim$100\,MFLOPS, 256\,KB SRAM)
\end{itemize}

All questions are grounded in the content and formulas of the \emph{Machine Learning Systems} textbook \citep{reddi2026mlsys, reddi2026a}, which provides the hardware specifications, architectural principles, and quantitative frameworks that serve as the foundation for question construction. The hardware constants are drawn from the MLSysIM physics engine \citep{reddi2026mlsysim}, ensuring that all questions reference the same specifications and that every napkin math answer can be independently verified against the simulator. This prevents specification drift --- a systematic error pattern where different questions cite different numbers for the same hardware, undermining the internal consistency that makes napkin math trustworthy. Hardware constants require maintenance as new accelerator generations ship; the methodology is designed to be timeless even as specific numbers evolve.

\subsection{Gap-Driven Construction}

Gap analysis identifies underfilled cells in the topic $\times$ zone $\times$ track space; construction campaigns target those cells explicitly. The coverage cube currently contains 289 semantically valid topic--track cells, of which 223 are healthy ($\geq 5$ questions) and 66 are underfilled (1--4 questions). Campaigns prioritize underfilled cells first, then level-balancing within healthy cells.

\subsection{Scaling via LLM-Assisted Generation}
\label{sec:llm-generation}

The contribution of \staffml{} is not the generation method but the \emph{quality infrastructure} that validates every question regardless of authorship. The corpus was scaled to over \numquestions{} questions using LLM-assisted generation (Gemini Pro and Claude) with structured prompts that inject the five grounding components above. In our quality analysis, we found no statistically significant difference in error rates between human-authored and LLM-generated questions after verification, confirming that the pipeline --- not the author --- determines question quality.

Each prompt specifies target coordinates (topic, zone, track, level), the zone description (which cognitive skills to exercise), the Bloom's level descriptor (which cognitive operation to target), and track-specific constraints (which physics regime applies). The textbook content seeds the prompts, ensuring questions test textbook-grounded reasoning rather than LLM-generated factoids. The approach is reproducible: the same prompt with the same hardware constants produces structurally equivalent questions, though specific scenarios vary.

The methodology treats LLMs as \emph{structured content generators}, not as domain experts. The quantitative grounding comes from injected constants and schema constraints, not from the model's training data. Three properties make the generated questions robust. First, every question requires reasoning from specific hardware constants that the candidate must internalize, not simply retrieve from a prompt. Second, the multi-model verification pipeline (\Cref{sec:qa}) catches factual errors and arithmetic mistakes that LLMs introduce. Third, the depth chain structure (\Cref{sec:chains}) ensures questions form coherent cognitive progressions that resist template-based generation.

Every generated question undergoes the same three-stage quality assurance as human-authored questions: schema validation ensures structural correctness, deduplication removes redundant items, and multi-model math verification independently recomputes every calculation from first principles (\Cref{sec:qa}). Community contribution of industry-sourced questions is planned to complement the generated corpus with production failure modes that LLM-generated scenarios may not capture.

\section{Quality Assurance}
\label{sec:qa}

Quality assurance operates at multiple stages. Every question must pass schema validation, deduplication, and math verification before entering the corpus. Cross-file structural consistency is enforced by a \numinvariants{}-check invariant system. This section describes each stage in detail.

\begin{figure}[t!]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{fig-quality-pipeline.pdf}
\caption{\textbf{Multi-stage quality assurance pipeline.} Candidate questions pass through schema validation (Pydantic), three-stage deduplication (exact, fuzzy, semantic), and multi-model math verification (Gemini Flash broad review + Claude Opus deep review). Confirmed errors are corrected; duplicates are archived, never deleted.}
\label{fig:quality-pipeline}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Schema Validation}

Every question is validated against a strict Pydantic schema (generated from the LinkML definition) with both field-level and cross-question rules:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Field constraints.} Minimum character lengths (scenario $\geq 50$, napkin\_math $\geq 10$ and must contain a digit), MCQ must have exactly 4 options with correct\_index in $[0,3]$
  \item \textbf{Taxonomy constraints.} topic $\in$ \{\numtopics{} curated IDs\}, zone $\in$ \{\numzones{} ikigai zones\}, track $\in$ \{\numtracks{} tracks\}, level $\in$ \{L1\ldots L6+\}
  \item \textbf{Uniqueness constraints.} No duplicate IDs, no duplicate (track, title) pairs among published questions
  \item \textbf{Linguistic checks.} No trailing quotes, no markdown formatting artifacts, no placeholder text
\end{itemize}

Field coverage across the corpus is 100\% for all required fields: topic, zone, competency\_area, napkin\_math, common\_mistake, realistic\_solution, and bloom\_level.

\subsection{Multi-Stage Deduplication}
\label{sec:dedup}

We employ a three-stage deduplication pipeline inspired by web-scale techniques \citep{broder1997minhash}:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Exact match.} Lowercased, whitespace-stripped scenario comparison. Catches copy-paste duplicates.
  \item \textbf{Fuzzy match.} \texttt{difflib.SequenceMatcher} $> 0.90$ within same (track, topic) groups. Catches minor rewordings.
  \item \textbf{Semantic match.} Sentence-BERT cosine similarity $> 0.85$ via ChromaDB. Catches same-concept-different-words duplicates.
\end{enumerate}

Confirmed duplicates are \emph{archived} (status set to ``archived''), never deleted, preserving the audit trail --- a practice adopted from large-scale community assessment management \citep{anking2024}.

\subsection{Math Verification}
\label{sec:math-review}

Napkin math is the soul of \staffml{} and its most error-prone component. Hardware specifications change across generations, unit conversions cascade, and order-of-magnitude errors are easy to introduce. We verify math through multi-model cross-review:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Round 1} (broad). Gemini Flash reviews all questions in 50-question chunks, checking arithmetic, hardware specs, unit consistency, and logical coherence.
  \item \textbf{Round 2} (deep). Claude Opus reviews the same questions independently, catching subtle logical errors that Flash misses.
  \item \textbf{Round 3} (exhaustive). 19 parallel Claude Opus agents, each with track-specific hardware reference sheets, independently recompute \emph{every} calculation from first principles.
  \item \textbf{Discrepancy analysis.} Errors found by one model but not the other are flagged for manual review. False positives are tracked to calibrate reviewer accuracy.
\end{enumerate}

Multi-model cross-review identified and corrected systematic errors including hardware specification inaccuracies, arithmetic mistakes, and unit conversion errors. The corpus includes a feedback mechanism for community error reporting post-publication. Recent work on LLM-as-judge evaluation \citep{zheng2023judging} provides additional evidence that multi-model agreement improves assessment reliability over single-model review.

After three rounds, the confirmed error rate among verified questions is below 0.05\%. Currently 66.2\% of questions have completed the full multi-model verification pipeline; the remaining 33.8\% have passed schema validation and deduplication but await deep math review. The reported error rate applies only to the verified subset and should not be extrapolated to the unverified portion.

\subsection{Structural Invariants}

Beyond per-question validation, \staffml{} enforces \numinvariants{} structural invariants across the three data files (corpus, taxonomy, chains). The invariant system enforces four categories of structural constraints:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Taxonomy integrity.} No duplicate topics or IDs, all IDs are kebab-case, prerequisite graph is acyclic, all referenced prerequisite targets exist
  \item \textbf{Classification consistency.} Every question has a valid topic (from the \numtopics{} curated set), zone (from \numzones{}), track (from \numtracks{}), and level (from \numlevels{}). No non-canonical values accepted
  \item \textbf{Coverage health.} All zones and topics populated above minimum thresholds, no topic exceeds 10\% of corpus, no singleton concepts
  \item \textbf{Cross-file coherence.} Chain question IDs exist in corpus, validation status consistent with validated flag, taxonomy question counts match corpus, no duplicate (track, title) pairs
\end{itemize}

These checks run as a CI gate --- no commit to the corpus is accepted if any invariant fails. The system catches errors that no single-question validator can detect, such as a topic deleted from the taxonomy but still referenced by 50 questions, a prerequisite cycle introduced by a new edge, or a topic that has accumulated an outsized share of questions.

\section{Example Questions}
\label{sec:examples}

This section presents five questions that illustrate different zones, tracks, and levels, showing how the four-axis classification captures qualitatively different cognitive acts. Each example demonstrates a distinct point in the classification space.

\subsection{Fluency: KV-Cache Sizing}

Fluency tests whether a candidate can perform napkin math from memory, recalling both the formula and the hardware specifications, then executing the arithmetic. It is the signature skill tested in Staff-level phone screens.

\begin{quote}
\footnotesize
\textbf{Topic}: \texttt{vram-budgeting} \quad \textbf{Zone}: fluency \\
\textbf{Track}: cloud \quad \textbf{Level}: L3

\textbf{Scenario}: You need to increase your LLM's context window from 4K to 128K tokens. The model weights fit perfectly in your 80\,GB VRAM. What hidden memory cost will cause your node to OOM during generation?

\textbf{Common mistake}: ``Activations will use more memory.'' Activations matter during training, but during inference the dominant hidden cost is something else entirely.

\textbf{Napkin math}: For Llama-70B (80 layers, 8 KV-heads via GQA, head\_dim=128) at 128K tokens in FP16:
\[
\text{KV-cache} = 2 \times 80 \times 8 \times 128 \times 128{,}000 \times 2 \approx \textbf{41.9\,GB}
\]
Combined with 140\,GB of weights, one 128K-context request requires $\sim$182\,GB, at least 3 H100s.

\textbf{Answer}: The KV-cache. It grows linearly with sequence length and, at 128K context, can exceed the model weights themselves.
\end{quote}

This is a \emph{fluency} question. The candidate must recall the KV-cache formula and execute the arithmetic from memory, producing a specific number. No architectural design is required; no tradeoff analysis is asked for. The cognitive act is ``napkin math from memory,'' the signature skill of the fluency zone.

\emph{Timeless principle: KV-cache memory scales linearly with sequence length and quadratically with attention head count. The specific H100 numbers change; the scaling law does not.}

\subsection{Diagnosis: MFU Collapse}

Diagnosis tests whether a candidate can identify root causes from observable symptoms. The candidate must recall hardware specifications \emph{and} reason about which bottleneck explains the observed behavior --- the ``3\,AM on-call'' skill.

\begin{quote}
\footnotesize
\textbf{Topic}: \texttt{roofline-analysis} \quad \textbf{Zone}: diagnosis \\
\textbf{Track}: cloud \quad \textbf{Level}: L4

\textbf{Scenario}: Your production LLM serving cluster reports 40\% MFU during the decode phase. The H100s are rated at 495\,TFLOPS FP16 dense. Your team assumes the GPUs are underclocked. Diagnose the actual root cause.

\textbf{Common mistake}: Blaming hardware or driver issues. The decode phase of autoregressive generation is inherently memory-bandwidth-bound, not compute-bound.

\textbf{Napkin math}: During decode, each token requires reading the full model weights ($2 \times 70\text{B} = 140\,\text{GB}$ in FP16) but performs only $2 \times 70\text{B} = 140$ GFLOPs of compute. Arithmetic intensity $= 140\,\text{GFLOPs} / 140\,\text{GB} = 1\,\text{FLOP/Byte}$, far below the H100's ridge point of $\sim$148\,FLOPs/Byte (dense). The workload is bound by the 3.35\,TB/s memory bandwidth ceiling, not the 495\,TFLOPS compute ceiling.

\textbf{Answer}: The decode phase is memory-bandwidth-bound. MFU measures compute utilization, but the bottleneck is data movement. The 40\% figure is not a deficiency; the GPUs are performing as expected given the workload's arithmetic intensity.
\end{quote}

This is a \emph{diagnosis} question. The candidate must recall hardware specifications (H100 bandwidth, ridge point) \emph{and} analyze why those specs explain the observed symptom (low MFU). The cognitive act is ``identify and explain a failure from first principles.''

\emph{Timeless principle: When arithmetic intensity falls below the hardware ridge point, adding compute does not help. Workloads shift from compute-bound to memory-bound as batch size decreases.}

\subsection{Specification: Latency-Constrained Serving}

Specification tests whether a candidate can translate quantitative constraints into a concrete architecture. The candidate must recall hardware capabilities \emph{and} design a system that satisfies a hard performance requirement --- the core skill of system design interviews.

\begin{quote}
\footnotesize
\textbf{Topic}: \texttt{model-serving-infrastructure} \quad \textbf{Zone}: specification \\
\textbf{Track}: cloud \quad \textbf{Level}: L5

\textbf{Scenario}: Your team must serve a 70B-parameter LLM with P99 time-to-first-token (TTFT) under 200\,ms. The model is deployed on H100 GPUs with NVLink. Specify the minimum serving configuration.

\textbf{Common mistake}: Focusing only on model parallelism degree while ignoring the prefill compute budget. A 70B model's prefill for a 2{,}000-token prompt requires $2 \times 70\text{B} \times 2{,}000 = 280$ TFLOPs.

\textbf{Napkin math}: A single H100 at 495\,TFLOPS FP16 dense completes the prefill in $280 / 495 \approx 566\,\text{ms}$, far exceeding the 200\,ms SLO. With 4-way tensor parallelism, prefill drops to $\sim$142\,\text{ms}, fitting the budget with margin for scheduling overhead. Model weights (140\,GB FP16) require 2 GPUs minimum for capacity. The configuration is 4$\times$ H100 with tensor parallelism, continuous batching, and chunked prefill.

\textbf{Answer}: 4-way tensor parallelism across H100s with NVLink. The constraint is prefill compute, not memory capacity: even though the model fits on 2 GPUs, the latency SLO demands additional parallelism.
\end{quote}

This is a \emph{specification} question. The candidate must recall hardware capabilities (H100 TFLOPS, NVLink bandwidth) \emph{and} design a serving architecture that satisfies a quantitative constraint (P99 TTFT $<$ 200\,ms). The cognitive act is ``translate constraints into architecture.''

\subsection{Mastery: Continuous Batching Stall}

Mastery requires all four skills simultaneously: recalling specs, analyzing the failure mode, designing an architectural fix, and sizing the solution with napkin math. These questions simulate the complete Staff-level interview loop.

\begin{quote}
\footnotesize
\textbf{Topic}: \texttt{batching-strategies} \quad \textbf{Zone}: mastery \\
\textbf{Track}: cloud \quad \textbf{Level}: L6+

\textbf{Scenario}: You implement continuous batching for an LLM endpoint. Under low load, TTFT is 100\,ms. Under high load, throughput triples, but TTFT spikes to over 4 seconds even though token generation speed remains fast. Diagnose the root cause, design a fix, and estimate the resource cost of your solution.

\textbf{Common mistake}: Assuming that because continuous batching optimizes throughput, it automatically guarantees low latency for incoming requests.

\textbf{Napkin math}: A 2,000-token prefill on a 70B model: $2 \times 70\text{B} \times 2{,}000 = 280$ TFLOPs. An H100 at 495\,TFLOPS dense needs $\sim$566\,ms. If 4 users send prompts simultaneously, the 4th waits over 1.5 seconds before prefill even begins.

\textbf{Answer}: Prefill starvation. The scheduler must chunk or defer prefills to prevent blocking the decode phase, but aggressive deferral under high load creates a queue that destroys TTFT. The fix is chunked prefill with priority scheduling, but this requires over-provisioning compute by $\sim$30\% to absorb the prefill bursts without queuing.
\end{quote}

This is a \emph{mastery} question. The candidate must recall hardware specs (H100 TFLOPS), analyze the scheduling conflict (prefill blocks decode), design a solution (chunked prefill with priority), and execute the napkin math to size the required over-provisioning. All four skills are exercised in a single question.

\emph{Timeless principle: In multiplexed serving, prefill and decode compete for the same compute; any scheduling policy must bound prefill latency to prevent decode starvation.}

\subsection{Cross-Track Comparison: Quantization}

The same topic, quantization, manifests differently across physics regimes, illustrating why the track axis is essential:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Cloud} (L4, evaluation). ``GPTQ vs.\ AWQ for serving a 70B model on 2$\times$ H100s. Which quantization scheme yields higher throughput at FP16-equivalent perplexity? Justify with arithmetic intensity analysis.'' The candidate evaluates two compression strategies against datacenter-scale metrics (tokens/second/dollar).
  \item \textbf{Edge} (L3, fluency). ``Quantize a YOLOv8 detection model from FP32 to INT8 for Orin deployment. Calculate the memory savings and estimate the latency improvement given Orin's INT8 TOPS vs.\ FP32 TFLOPS.'' The candidate performs napkin math within a thermal and real-time envelope.
  \item \textbf{TinyML} (L5, specification). ``Your 256\,KB MCU must run a keyword spotting model. The FP32 model is 800\,KB. Specify a quantization strategy (INT8, INT4, or binary) that fits in SRAM while maintaining $>$90\% accuracy. Justify each precision choice for weights vs.\ activations.'' The candidate designs a mixed-precision scheme under extreme memory constraints.
\end{itemize}

All three questions test quantization knowledge, but the physics regime determines the constraints, the formulas, and the engineering tradeoffs. A candidate who can answer the cloud question may struggle with the TinyML variant, because the reasoning transfers only partially across regimes.

\bigskip

These five questions demonstrate how the four-axis classification captures qualitatively different cognitive acts across the ML systems engineering domain. Fluency tests whether you can \emph{compute} a number. Diagnosis tests whether you can \emph{explain} an observation. Specification tests whether you can \emph{translate constraints into architecture}. Mastery tests whether you can \emph{synthesize} a complete solution. The cross-track comparison shows that the same topic demands different reasoning in different physics regimes.

Future work includes questions that provide partial information, requiring candidates to identify what additional data they need --- a skill that tests meta-cognitive reasoning about systems diagnostics. Such incomplete-information questions would assess whether candidates know what they do not know, a hallmark of Staff-level engineering judgment.

\section{Practical Applications}
\label{sec:practical}

The zone model translates directly into interview loop design. A typical four-round interview should cover at least four zones to assess breadth of systems reasoning:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Phone screen.} Fluency questions. Can the candidate perform napkin math from memory? This is the fastest signal for mechanical sympathy.
  \item \textbf{System design.} Specification or Design questions. Can the candidate architect a system under quantitative constraints?
  \item \textbf{Debugging.} Diagnosis questions. Can the candidate root-cause a failure from observable symptoms and hardware specifications?
  \item \textbf{Architecture review.} Evaluation or Mastery questions. Can the candidate analyze tradeoffs and justify design decisions with quantitative reasoning?
\end{itemize}

A ``strong hire'' signal at Staff level is consistent strength in compound zones, especially diagnosis and evaluation. A ``no hire'' signal is strong recall but weakness across all compound zones --- the profile of a memorizer rather than a reasoner. No interview loop should test only pure zones, as these fail to exercise the integrative reasoning that distinguishes Staff-level engineers from mid-level ones.

Not all 11 zones are equally important for interview design. The five compound zones (diagnosis, specification, fluency, evaluation, and optimization) and mastery carry the most signal for Staff+ assessment. The four pure zones (recall, analyze, design, quantify) serve primarily as corpus organization categories and warm-up question pools; they are the building blocks from which compound zones are constructed, not assessment targets in their own right. Realization (design $\cap$ quantify), while theoretically defined, appears infrequently in practice because most sizing questions also require recall, making them fluency or specification questions instead.

\subsection{ML Systems Research}

The technical content of \staffml{} questions is grounded in seminal ML systems research. Megatron-LM \citep{shoeybi2019megatron} introduced tensor parallelism for training large language models, providing the foundation for questions about parallelism strategies and communication overhead. GPipe \citep{huang2019gpipe} established pipeline parallelism and the bubble fraction analysis that appears in realization-zone questions. ZeRO \citep{rajbhandari2020zero} and DeepSpeed \citep{rasley2020deepspeed} eliminated memory redundancy in data parallelism, motivating questions about optimizer state partitioning and memory budgeting.

FlashAttention \citep{dao2022flashattention} demonstrated IO-aware algorithm design, reducing memory reads/writes from quadratic to linear in sequence length, and provides the canonical example of how understanding the memory hierarchy yields algorithmic improvements invisible to complexity analysis alone. vLLM \citep{kwon2023vllm} introduced PagedAttention for KV-cache management, directly inspiring our fluency and diagnosis questions about memory fragmentation and serving throughput. Orca \citep{yu2022orca} pioneered continuous batching (iteration-level scheduling), which motivates our mastery-zone questions about prefill starvation and scheduling tradeoffs.

MLPerf \citep{mattson2020mlperf, reddi2020mlperf} established standardized benchmarks for measuring ML \emph{system} performance: training time, inference latency, and throughput across hardware platforms. The distinction between MLPerf and \staffml{} is important. MLPerf measures how well systems perform; \staffml{} measures how well \emph{humans} reason about systems performance. MLPerf's measurement methodology (standardized workloads, reproducible configurations, hardware-specific baselines) informs the quantitative grounding of \staffml{} questions, and several questions directly reference MLPerf benchmarking scenarios. However, the assessment targets are fundamentally different --- MLPerf evaluates hardware and software implementations, while \staffml{} evaluates human engineering judgment about those implementations.

\subsection{Educational Measurement}

The assessment design methodology draws on three traditions. Bloom's revised taxonomy \citep{anderson2001taxonomy, bloom1956taxonomy} provides the six-level cognitive framework that maps to our mastery levels, validated by decades of educational research as a reliable predictor of assessment difficulty \citep{webb1997dok, biggs1982solo}. Evidence-Centered Design \citep{mislevy2003ecd} provides the three-component assessment architecture (claim, evidence, task features) that structures our construction pipeline. Automatic Item Generation \citep{gierl2013aig, aig_llm_2024} provides the theoretical foundation for template-based question generation, extended here with hardware-grounded construction and multi-model verification.

The topic taxonomy follows SKOS \citep{skos2009} for knowledge organization, faceted classification principles \citep{hjorland2013facet} for orthogonal axis design, and test blueprinting methodology \citep{millman1989, case2002constructing} for coverage validation. Item Response Theory \citep{lord1980irt, hambleton1991irt} provides the psychometric framework for future difficulty calibration once sufficient response data ($\geq$30 responses per item) is collected.

The TinyTorch educational framework \citep{reddi2026tinytorch} provides a complementary pedagogical approach: where \staffml{} assesses whether engineers \emph{have} ML systems competencies, TinyTorch teaches those competencies through a build-from-scratch curriculum spanning 20 modules.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

We identify four limitations of the current work.

\textbf{No empirical validation.} The question bank has been editorially validated through multi-model cross-review but has not yet been psychometrically validated through user studies. Correlating zone-level performance with engineering seniority is an important next step. Until response data is collected, zone classifications, level assignments, and difficulty orderings reflect expert judgment rather than empirical measurement of candidate performance.

\textbf{Static questions with chain-based progression.} While individual questions are static text, the depth chain structure enables adaptive progression: a candidate who answers an L3 question correctly is presented with the L4 question on the same topic. This simulates some of the adaptivity of live interviews, though it does not replicate the free-form dialogue of whiteboard sessions. The chains (\Cref{sec:chains}) approximate the probing behavior of experienced interviewers.

\textbf{Hardware vendor concentration.} The corpus draws predominantly on NVIDIA hardware specifications (H100, A100, Orin), reflecting current industry adoption. Candidates with primary experience on AMD, Intel, or Google TPU hardware may find the specific constants unfamiliar, though the reasoning patterns (roofline analysis, memory budgeting, power-performance tradeoffs) are vendor-neutral. Future versions will include multi-vendor question variants. For assessment contexts, we recommend providing a hardware specification reference sheet alongside the questions, ensuring that recall of vendor-specific numbers does not gate access to the reasoning being tested.

\textbf{Hardware constant maintenance.} Every question is grounded in specific hardware specifications (H100: 80\,GB HBM3, 3.35\,TB/s, 495\,TFLOPS FP16 dense; Orin: 275\,TOPS INT8). These numbers are correct as of the current accelerator generation but require updates as new hardware ships. The shared physics engine centralizes constants to make updates tractable. Changing one constant propagates to all affected questions, and the invariant system flags any question whose napkin math references outdated specifications. The methodology is designed to be timeless even as specific numbers evolve.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
\label{sec:conclusion}

\staffml{} introduces a structured approach to assessing ML systems engineering expertise. The core contribution is the \emph{ikigai competency model}, which decomposes systems expertise into four fundamental skills (recall, analyze, design, quantify) whose pairwise intersections produce eleven cognitive zones that capture qualitatively different acts of engineering reasoning. This model, combined with questions that require napkin math against real hardware specifications, provides a vocabulary for what it means to ``know'' ML systems engineering at each level of professional seniority.

The four-axis classification framework (topic, zone, track, level) operationalizes this competency model into a practical assessment system. The topic taxonomy organizes \numtopics{} concepts with prerequisite relationships that enable personalized study paths. The track axis grounds every question in a specific physics regime, ensuring that assessment reflects the real constraints of cloud, edge, mobile, and TinyML deployment. The level axis maps to both Bloom's cognitive taxonomy and industry engineering ladders, making \staffml{} actionable for candidates preparing for specific roles and hiring managers calibrating interview loops.

Empirical validation through user studies is the most important next step.

\subsection{Future Directions}

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{IRT calibration.} Once $n \geq 30$ responses per item are collected, Item Response Theory calibration will assign empirical difficulty and discrimination parameters.
  \item \textbf{Community contributions.} A contribution pipeline with automated schema validation will enable industry practitioners to submit questions grounded in real production failure modes.
  \item \textbf{Adaptive selection.} Question selection that adjusts to individual performance profiles across zones, targeting the zones where a candidate is weakest.
  \item \textbf{Incomplete-information questions.} Questions that provide partial data, requiring candidates to identify what additional information they need before solving the problem.
\end{itemize}

The prerequisite graph also enables graph-guided study paths that sequence topics based on dependency structure rather than arbitrary ordering.

The corpus, schema, and quality infrastructure are available as a companion to the \emph{Machine Learning Systems} textbook \citep{reddi2026mlsys}\footnote{\url{https://staffml.dev}}.
